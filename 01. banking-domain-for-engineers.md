# The Banking Domain for Engineers

**A Domain Guide to Retail, Lending, and Wealth for Software Engineers**

*We map the whole of banking, then go deep into wealth.*

Sourav

# Preface

A bank looks, at first, like a large pile of products. There are checking accounts and savings accounts. There are mortgages, car loans, and credit cards. There are brokerage accounts, retirement accounts, and managed portfolios. Listed together, they seem unrelated. For an engineer who has just joined this world, that pile is a hard place to begin.

Most writing about the banking domain does not help with this. It is written for business analysts, for product managers, or for compliance teams. It explains rules and processes. It rarely explains the domain to the people who build the software that runs it. Those people have a specific need. We turn the domain into data models, services, and screens. The shape of the domain becomes the shape of our code. So we need to understand that shape clearly, and for its own sake.

This book is written for us, the engineers. We take the whole banking domain and sort it into three categories of operation. We call them retail, lending, and wealth. Retail is the holding and moving of money. Lending is the giving of money now and the taking of it back over time. Wealth is the owning of assets whose value changes. Each has other common names, and we will meet them as we continue.

In Part 1 we give the three categories equal attention. We draw a map of the whole domain. After Part 1, we spend the rest of the book inside one category: wealth. We choose wealth because it is the hardest of the three to model well. Its truth does not sit inside the bank. It sits outside, in the market. It changes every second. And at many moments it is not even a single, settled number. Retail and lending are demanding in their own ways, but wealth adds a kind of difficulty the other two do not have. That difficulty is worth a whole book.

A word on how we write. We write as "we." The author and the reader explore the domain together. When we say "we build a model" or "we show a customer a balance," we mean the shared work of understanding and, later, of building.

A word, too, on what this book is not. It is not a book about software architecture. We describe the domain. We do not prescribe how to arrange code, draw boundaries, or choose patterns. That is a large subject, and it deserves its own book. Here we keep our attention on the domain itself, so that it stays in clear view.

# What We Need

We do not need a background in finance. We do not need to have worked inside a bank. Every idea in this book is explained from the start.

We do need to be comfortable with software engineering in a general way. We use words like data, state, record, and system, and we assume these words are familiar. We do not assume any one programming language, and we do not assume any one platform.

It also helps to be curious about how money systems work. Much of this domain is invisible in daily life. A payment "just works." A share price "just updates." We will look inside these events and see what is really happening.

A small number of code-like structures appear in the book. They show the shape of a piece of domain data. One might list the fields of an order, or the parts of a single purchase lot. These structures are not written in any real language. We can read them as plain descriptions of what the data holds. Reading them needs no tools and no setup. Nothing in this book needs to be installed or run.

# How This Book Is Organized

Part 1 draws the map. It has five chapters.

The first chapter presents the three categories and explains why we divide banking by what it does, rather than by the products it sells. The second chapter looks closely at retail. The third looks closely at lending. The fourth gives a broad survey of wealth, which opens the detailed part of the book. The fifth chapter looks at products that sit across more than one category, such as a card that both pays and lends. These crossing products test our map, and they turn our attention toward wealth.

From Part 2 onward, we stay inside wealth and go deep. We cover the kinds of accounts a customer can hold. We cover the instruments they can own, such as shares, funds, and bonds. We cover positions and portfolios, market data, the life of a trade from order to settlement, the measurement of performance, the handling of tax, the work of advice and planning, the rules that govern all of it, and the statements and reports that bring it into one view. Each of these is a large topic, and each gets enough space.

One pattern repeats through the whole book. For each new topic, we first say plainly what it is. Only then do we look closer. We meet the idea before we study it.

# Source Code

This is a domain book, so it carries very little code.

Where a structure does appear, it describes a domain object and nothing more. It is there to make an idea concrete. It is not meant to be compiled, and it is not meant to be run. We keep these structures free of language-specific detail on purpose. A field with a simple name and a simple type tells us what the domain holds. It does not tell us how to build a system around it.

The larger question — how to turn this domain into well-structured software, with clear layers and boundaries — is real and important. It is also a separate subject. We leave it for a later book, and we keep it out of these pages. Our goal here is to hold the domain still and look at it closely. Once we understand the domain, the work of building for it becomes far easier.

# Part 1: Retail, Lending, and Wealth

Before we go deep into any one area, we need a map of the whole. Part 1 builds that map. We sort every common banking activity into three categories. We learn to tell them apart. And we see why one of them, wealth, will take up the rest of the book.

# Chapter 1: One Domain, Three Categories

A retail-facing bank offers many products. The list is long, and it grows every year. Written out in full, the products look like a pile of unrelated things. This is a poor place for an engineer to start. Before we can model anything, we need a way to see the whole domain in order. In this chapter we build that order. We reduce the long list of products to a short list of operations, and we group the whole domain into three categories.

## Products Are Many; Operations Are Few

The product list of a bank is long by design. Products are how a bank presents itself to customers and to the market. A single product often combines several features. Products are renamed, replaced, and reshaped often. If we organize the domain by product, our map changes every time the marketing changes. That is not a stable foundation.

So we ask a different question. For each thing a bank offers, we ask what the bank is actually doing. When we look this way, the picture simplifies quickly. The number of truly distinct operations is small. For a bank that serves everyday customers, three operations cover almost everything.

The bank holds money for customers and moves it when they ask. We call this retail.

The bank lends money to customers and is repaid, with interest, over time. We call this lending.

The bank helps customers own assets whose value rises and falls. We call this wealth.

Each of these three has other names in common use. Retail is often called everyday banking. Lending is often called credit. Wealth is often called investment, or wealth management. All of these names are correct, and a reader will meet them elsewhere. In this book we lean on the first name in each pair. We say retail, lending, and wealth. We reach for the other names only now and then, so that they stay familiar.

The key move is this. We group the domain by the operation, not by the product. A product is a package. An operation is what happens inside it. As we will see in Chapter 5, a single product can contain more than one operation at once. Grouping by operation lets us handle that cleanly, instead of being confused by it.

## The Three Categories

We now define the three categories with a little more care. For each one, we name the defining act, the thing that makes it what it is. Later chapters fill in the detail. Here we only need clear definitions.

### Retail

In retail, the bank keeps customers' money safe and moves it on their instruction. A checking account and a savings account are retail. So is a payment to a shop, a transfer to a friend, and a direct debit for a bill. A debit card belongs here too, because using it simply moves money out of an account.

The defining act of retail is safekeeping and movement. Money comes in, money goes out, and money moves from place to place. The bank's job is to record all of this correctly and to keep the balance right.

### Lending

In lending, the bank gives the customer money now, and the customer returns it later, together with interest. A mortgage is lending. So is a car loan, a personal loan, and a student loan. The borrowing side of a credit card is lending as well.

The defining act of lending is money out now, repaid over time. The bank provides a sum today and expects a series of payments in return. Those payments are set out in advance, in an agreement between the bank and the customer.

### Wealth

In wealth, the customer's money is turned into assets that the customer owns, and the value of those assets changes over time. Buying shares in a company is wealth. So is holding a fund, owning bonds, and keeping a retirement account or a managed portfolio.

The defining act of wealth is owning something whose value moves. The customer no longer holds a fixed amount of money. They hold assets, and those assets are worth more on some days and less on others. The bank helps the customer buy, hold, sell, and keep track of them.

## Two Questions We Ask of Each Category

A list of three categories is a start, but it is not yet a map. To compare the categories in a way that teaches us something, we ask the same two questions of each. The answers will differ sharply, and those differences are the heart of Part 1.

### Where Does the Truth Live?

Whenever we show a customer a balance or a value, some source is the authority for that number. It is the place we trust when there is any doubt. We ask, for each category: where does that authority sit? Is it inside the bank, or outside it? Does it hold still, or does it move on its own?

### What Shape Do Money and State Take?

We also ask how money behaves in each category, and what we must remember as its current state. In one category the state might be a single balance. In another it might be a schedule that runs over months or years. In a third it might be a collection of holdings, each valued from the outside. The shape of the state tells us a great deal about what will be hard to build.

## The Three Categories Through Both Questions

With the two questions in hand, we look at each category again. This time we answer both questions for each, and a clear pattern appears.

In retail, the truth lives inside the bank, in its own ledger. The balance of an account is whatever the bank's records say it is. Nothing outside the bank sets it. Money behaves by moving: in, out, and between accounts. The state we keep is, at heart, a balance, together with the history of movements that produced it. The hard parts of retail come from this movement. We must apply events in the right order. We must avoid carrying out the same instruction twice by accident. We must keep every debit matched by a credit, so the books always agree.

In lending, the truth lives inside an agreement. When a loan begins, its terms fix a schedule of payments reaching into the future. The current state of the loan, how much is still owed and what is due next, follows from that schedule and the payments made so far. Money behaves in a clear arc. A sum goes out once, at the start, and returns in parts, with interest, over time. The hard parts of lending come from time. We must work out interest as it builds up day by day. We must decide what happens when a payment is late or missed.

In wealth, the truth is split in a way the other two categories never face. How much the customer holds, the number of shares or the number of units, is recorded inside the bank, in its custody records. But what those holdings are worth is set outside the bank, by the market. The bank does not choose the price. It receives it. Money behaves by changing form. Cash is turned into assets, and those assets carry a value that rises and falls on its own. The state we keep is a portfolio: a set of holdings whose worth is decided from outside and never stops moving. The hard parts of wealth come from this. We must be able to value the portfolio at any moment. We must measure how well it has done over time. We must track fine details of each purchase, because they matter later for tax.

We can gather all of this into a single view.

| Category | Where the truth lives | How money behaves | What we keep as state | The hard part |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Retail | Inside the bank, in its ledger | Moves in, out, and between accounts | A balance and its history of movements | Order of events, no double action, books that always agree |
| Lending | Inside an agreement and its schedule | Goes out once, returns in parts with interest | A repayment schedule that unfolds over time | Interest that builds up, and late or missed payments |
| Wealth | Split: quantity inside the bank, value outside in the market | Changes form, from cash into assets | A portfolio valued from outside, always moving | Valuing at any moment, measuring performance, tracking tax detail |

## Why Wealth Is the Hardest to Model

The table lets us see, all at once, why wealth stands apart.

In retail, the truth is inside the bank, and it holds still. A balance does not change until the bank itself records a movement. Between movements, it is fixed and certain.

In lending, the truth is also inside the bank, held in an agreement. It does change over time, but it changes in a way we can work out in advance. Given the terms of a loan, we can calculate the whole future schedule on the day it begins. The change is known ahead of time.

In wealth, none of this holds. Three properties set it apart, and together they make it the hardest of the three to model.

First, the truth is external. We do not set the value of a holding. The market sets it, and we can only receive what the market gives.

Second, the truth is live. A price can change every second the market is open. It can also change outside those hours, through events that reach the asset while trading is closed.

Third, the truth is uncertain. At many moments there is no single agreed price at all. There is the price of the last trade, which is already in the past. There is the highest price a buyer will pay and the lowest a seller will accept, and these two differ. There may be a feed that is delayed on purpose. Before we can show "the value," we must first decide what value even means in that moment.

These three properties, external, live, and uncertain, do not appear together anywhere in retail or lending. They are the reason wealth needs the depth of a whole book. From Part 2 onward, that is the depth we give it.

## Where We Go From Here

Choosing to spend the rest of the book on wealth does not mean we abandon retail and lending. It means we have given them the space they need for a map, and no more. We stop at the edge of each, because each could fill a book of its own. To go deep into retail, or deep into lending, would pull us away from our subject.

We keep them close for two reasons.

The first reason is contrast. Wealth's difficulty is only clear when we can see it next to something simpler. Retail and lending are that baseline. Against the still, certain balance of retail, and against the known schedule of a loan, the moving and uncertain value of a portfolio stands out plainly. Without the baseline, we would have nothing to measure that difficulty against.

The second reason is shared ground. The three categories are not separate from one another. Every one of them needs a way to know who the customer is. Every one needs a way to move money. Every one rests on some idea of an account. And every one must answer to rules and regulation. These shared parts mean wealth is not a separate island. When we reach it, we will find that it reuses much of what retail and lending already put in place. Understanding the map as a whole, even briefly, saves us from treating wealth as if it stood alone.

With the map drawn, we can look more closely at each category in turn. In Chapter 2 we study retail up close. In Chapter 3 we study lending. In Chapter 4 we take a broad survey of wealth, wide rather than deep, to prepare for the detailed chapters that follow. In Chapter 5 we return to the products that sit across more than one category, and we let them point us toward wealth, where the rest of the book begins.

# Chapter 2: Retail

Retail is the category most of us meet first. Before we ever build banking software, we are customers. We hold an account. We are paid into it. We spend from it. We move money to other people. All of this is retail. Its defining act, as we saw in Chapter 1, is safekeeping and movement. The bank keeps our money safe, and it moves that money when we ask.

In this chapter we look at what falls under retail. We start with the account, which holds the money. We move to the many ways money travels in, out, and across. Then we look closely at two things an engineer must understand well: the balance, which is not the single number it appears to be, and the ledger, which is where retail's truth lives. We finish with what makes retail hard, and with what retail gives to the rest of banking.

## The Account: A Place to Hold Money

At the center of retail sits the account. An account is a record of money that a customer owns and the bank holds. It is worth being exact about what that means. When money sits in a checking account, the bank does not keep those exact notes in a drawer with the customer's name on them. The balance is a promise. It is money the bank owes back to the customer, and the account is the record of that promise.

Retail offers a few kinds of account, and they differ mainly in what the customer wants from them.

A checking account is built for everyday use. Money arrives often, and money leaves often. A salary is paid in. Bills go out. Cards draw on it. Because it is used so heavily, a checking account usually pays little or no interest. Its value is convenience, not growth. In some countries this is called a current account.

A savings account is built for holding money rather than spending it. Money moves through it far less often. In return, it usually pays interest, because the customer is leaving money with the bank for longer. Some savings accounts limit how often money can be withdrawn.

A term deposit sits a step further in the same direction. Here the customer agrees to leave a sum with the bank for a fixed period, and the bank pays a higher rate in return. The money is locked for the term. In the United States this is often called a certificate of deposit, or CD. It is still retail, because the bank is still holding the customer's money and owes it back. The only new idea is time. The customer promises to wait, and the bank pays more for that promise.

Across all of these, the pattern is the same. An account belongs to a customer. It holds a balance. That balance is money the bank owes. What changes from one account type to the next is how freely the money moves and how much the bank pays to hold it.

## Moving Money

If the account is where money rests, movement is where most of retail happens. Most of what a retail system does, day to day, is move money correctly. We can look at this in two ways: the directions money travels, and the systems that carry it.

### In, Out, and Across

Money moves in three basic directions.

Money moves in. A salary is paid into the account. A person deposits cash or a check. Someone sends money from another account. We call these deposits, or credits, because they increase the balance.

Money moves out. Cash is taken from a machine. A card pays a shop. A bill is collected. We call these withdrawals, or debits, because they reduce the balance.

Money moves across. The customer sends money from their account to another account. That other account may be their own, or another person's, or one at a different bank. From the sending account this looks like money out. From the receiving account it looks like money in. A transfer is really two movements that must agree.

Every one of these is a movement that the bank must record exactly and reflect in the balance. The account, on its own, is simple. The movements are where the care is needed.

### The Rails That Carry Money

When money moves across, something has to carry it from one place to another. The systems that do this carrying are often called payment rails. A payment rail is the network that actually moves the money and tells each side that it has moved. Retail uses several rails, and they differ in speed, in cost, and in whether a payment can be undone. An engineer benefits from telling them apart, because the choice of rail shapes what the software must handle.

Card networks carry card payments. When a card pays a shop, a network such as Visa or Mastercard checks in real time that the money is there and approves the payment. The actual money changes hands a little later, in batches. We will return to this two-step nature soon, because it explains one of the balance's puzzles.

Batch transfer systems carry large numbers of ordinary payments together. In the United States this system is called the Automated Clearing House, or ACH. It gathers payments and processes them in cycles rather than one at a time. It is cheap, and it is used for salaries, for bill payments, and for transfers between accounts. It is not instant. A payment may take a day, sometimes less. Within limits, some of these payments can be reversed.

Wire transfers carry single, high-value payments quickly and with finality. A wire moves a specific sum from one bank to another and settles it firmly. It is fast, and it is expensive, and once sent it is very hard to undo. Wires are used when the amount is large and the payment must be certain.

Instant payment systems carry payments that clear in seconds, at any hour. In the United States these include newer systems built for exactly this purpose. Many countries have their own. A payment on such a rail is fast and final. It arrives at once, and it cannot be reversed. This finality is a feature for the person receiving money, and a risk for the person sending it by mistake.

Checks are the oldest rail still in common use in some countries. A check is a paper instruction to pay. It must be handed over, deposited, and cleared, which takes time. Checks are slowly becoming rare, but a retail system in many markets must still handle them.

We do not need to master every rule of every rail here. We only need the map. Different rails move money at different speeds, at different costs, and with different rules about reversal. The debit card fits neatly into this map. It uses the card network to draw money straight from the account. The credit card looks similar when it pays, but its money is borrowed rather than drawn from a balance. That makes the credit card a product that lives in two categories at once, and we save it for Chapter 5.

## The Balance and the Ledger

We now come to two ideas that an engineer must hold clearly, because they are easy to get wrong. The first is that an account does not have one balance. The second is that the balance is not the real truth at all. Beneath it sits the ledger.

### More Than One Balance

It is natural to think an account holds a single number. In practice, at any moment, an account can show more than one balance, and they can differ.

The clearest example uses two of them. One is the posted balance, sometimes called the ledger balance. This is the balance of money that has fully settled. Every movement counted in it is complete. The other is the available balance. This is the money the customer can actually use right now.

These two differ whenever something is in progress. Suppose an account has one hundred in settled money. The customer uses a card to pay thirty at a shop. The card network approves the payment at once, and the bank places a hold on thirty. That thirty has not yet left the account in the settled sense. So the posted balance still reads one hundred, while the available balance reads seventy. A day or two later the payment settles. Only then does the posted balance fall to seventy as well, and the hold is released.

Holds work in the other direction too. When a customer deposits a check, the bank may show the money in the posted balance but not release all of it for use straight away. It waits to be sure the check is good. During that wait, the posted balance can be higher than the available balance.

For a customer, the difference between these balances is a common source of confusion. For an engineer, it is a basic fact of the domain. The question "what is the balance?" has more than one correct answer. We must always know which one a screen, a rule, or a decision is asking for.

### The Ledger Beneath the Balance

A balance, of either kind, is not something the bank simply stores and edits. It is the result of a longer record. That record is the ledger, and it is where retail's truth lives.

The ledger works on a rule that is centuries old: double entry. Every movement of money is written as balanced entries, and the entries must always sum to zero. When money leaves one place, it must arrive somewhere else. Nothing is created, and nothing vanishes. If thirty leaves a customer's account, thirty must be recorded as arriving elsewhere, such as at the shop's bank. The two entries balance.

What the customer sees as a single line, for example "shop, thirty out," is, inside the bank, part of a balanced pair. The customer sees their side. The ledger records both sides, so that the books always agree.

A single entry in such a ledger holds a small, fixed set of facts. We can sketch its shape.

```
LedgerEntry
  entryId       a unique name for this entry
  accountId     the account the entry belongs to
  amount        how much money moved
  direction     debit or credit
  status        pending or posted
  occurredAt    when the movement happened
  description   the text the customer reads
```

This is not code to run. It is a plain description of what one entry holds. The fields tell us what the domain cares about: which account, how much, in which direction, at what stage, and when. The balance we spoke of earlier is not stored on its own. It is what we get when we take the entries for an account and add them up. The posted balance uses the posted entries. The available balance uses the posted entries and then adjusts for holds.

This is why we say the ledger is the source of truth. The balance is a summary. The ledger is the record the summary comes from. When the two ever disagree, the ledger is right, and the balance must be corrected to match it.

## What Makes Retail Hard

Retail can look simple. Money goes in, money goes out, and a balance changes. The difficulty is not in any single movement. It is in doing a very large number of movements, for a very large number of accounts, without ever making a mistake with money. Three problems capture most of this difficulty.

The first is order. Movements must be applied in a consistent order, because the order can change the result. Imagine an account with a small balance. On the same day, a large payment goes out and a deposit comes in. If the deposit is applied first, the account stays positive. If the payment is applied first, the account may briefly go negative, which can trigger a fee. The movements are the same. Only their order differs, and the outcome for the customer is not. A retail system must decide the order of movements clearly and apply it the same way every time.

The second is doing each movement once, and only once. Messages between systems can be lost and sent again. A request to pay can arrive twice. But the money must move a single time. If the same payment were applied twice, the customer would lose money that they never spent. If a deposit were applied twice, the bank would hand out money that never arrived. Neither can be allowed. The domain demands that each real movement of money happens exactly once, however many times a message about it is delivered.

The third is keeping money whole. Across the whole system, money must be conserved. Every debit must be matched by a credit. No process may quietly create money, and none may lose it. This is why the ledger balances by design, and why every account and every movement must reconcile. At the end of any period, the sum of what the bank holds must match the sum of what it owes. When it does not, something is wrong, and it must be found.

There is a fourth point that follows from all of these. In retail we do not undo a mistake by erasing it. A ledger entry, once posted, is not deleted. If a movement was wrong, we post a new entry that reverses it. The record grows; it is not rewritten. This keeps the history honest, so that we can always see what happened and why the balance is what it is. The past is corrected by adding to it, never by hiding it.

## The Foundation Retail Provides

We have given retail enough attention to hold it as a clear picture. We stop here, at the edge of the subject, because retail alone could fill an entire book. Interest rules, overdraft handling, fraud checks, and the fine detail of each rail all lie beyond our map. For our purpose, the picture is enough.

Before we leave, it is worth naming what retail builds that the rest of banking will reuse. This matters, because when we reach wealth, we do not want to treat it as if it stood alone. It does not. Much of its ground is laid here.

Retail establishes the idea of a customer, and a way to know who that customer is. It establishes the account as a container that belongs to someone. It establishes money movement, and the rails that carry it. And it establishes the ledger, and with it the habit of recording every movement so that money is always conserved. Lending will build on these. Wealth will build on them too.

There is one more thing retail gives us, and it is the reason it comes first in Part 1. Retail is the certain baseline. Its truth sits inside the bank, in the ledger, and between movements it holds perfectly still. A balance does not change on its own. It changes only when the bank records a movement, and the moment it does, the new balance is exact and final. We will carry this sense of a still, certain, internal truth with us. It is the thing against which wealth, whose truth sits outside and never stops moving, will look so different. In the next chapter we turn to lending, where the truth is still inside the bank, but where, for the first time, it begins to move with time.
