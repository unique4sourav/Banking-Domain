# Chapter 4: Wealth

We reach the third category, and the one this book is written for. Chapter 3 left us with a promise about it. In wealth, value moves on its own, as it did in lending, but we can no longer know that movement in advance. The truth of wealth does not sit inside the bank. It sits outside, in the market, and the bank can only receive it. This is the difficulty we first named in Chapter 1. Wealth's truth is external, it is live, and it is uncertain.

This chapter is a survey. Its job is to show us the whole of wealth at once, so that we hold a map of the territory before we enter it. We keep it wide rather than deep. For each part of wealth, we say plainly what it is, and then we move on. The close study of each part is the work of the chapters that follow. In a sense, the sections of this chapter are the plan for the rest of the book. Each one names a place we will later return to and explore fully.

We will move in a natural order. First we ask where wealth is held. Then we ask what is owned, and what we hold as a result. We ask what those holdings are worth, and how ownership changes when we buy and sell. We ask how we measure how well we have done, and what we owe in tax. We ask how advice and planning fit in, and what rules govern all of it. Finally we ask how the whole picture is brought together and shown.

## Where Wealth Is Held: Accounts

As in retail, wealth begins with an account. But a wealth account is not quite like a retail account. A retail account holds a balance of money. A wealth account holds assets that the customer owns, and often some cash beside them. The account is still a container that belongs to a customer, but what it contains is different.

Wealth accounts come in a few main kinds, and they differ most in their tax treatment and their rules.

A brokerage account is the plainest kind. The customer places money in it and uses that money to buy and hold investments. Gains and income in a brokerage account are usually taxed as they arise. There are few special limits.

A retirement account is built to help people save for later life, and it carries special tax treatment in return for special rules. In the United States, common kinds include the Individual Retirement Account, or IRA, and the 401k, which is offered through an employer. Some of these are funded with money before tax and taxed later, when it is withdrawn. Others, such as a Roth IRA, are funded with money after tax and are not taxed again when it is withdrawn. In exchange for these advantages, retirement accounts limit how much can be added each year and when money can be taken out without penalty. When money moves from one retirement account to another, for example when a person changes jobs, this is called a rollover.

A managed or advisory account is one where the customer hands day-to-day decisions to a professional or to a program. The customer still owns the assets, but someone else, or something else, chooses and adjusts them. These arrangements come in several forms, with names such as separately managed accounts and wrap accounts, and increasingly as automated services often called robo-advisers.

We do not need the fine detail of each account type now. We only need to see that the account type is not a small matter in wealth. It shapes how gains are taxed, how much can be saved, and who makes the decisions. We will return to accounts in depth later.

## What Is Owned: Instruments

Inside a wealth account, money is used to buy things the customer then owns. These things are called instruments, or assets. There are several broad kinds, and each behaves in its own way. Here we name them and say briefly what each is.

Equities are shares in a company. To own a share is to own a small part of that company. Shares are also called stocks. Their value rises and falls with the fortunes of the company and the mood of the market.

Funds are pooled investments. Instead of buying many things one by one, the customer buys a single fund, and the fund holds a basket of underlying assets. A mutual fund is one common form. An exchange-traded fund, or ETF, is another, and it can be bought and sold during the trading day much like a share. Funds let a customer own a wide spread of assets in a single holding.

Fixed income means bonds and similar instruments. A bond is, in essence, a loan made tradable. The buyer lends money to a government or a company and receives regular interest and, later, the return of the sum. Here we can see lending from the last chapter appear again, this time as something a customer can own and sell.

Options and other derivatives are instruments whose value comes from something else, such as a share. They are more advanced, and they carry more risk. We name them here and leave their detail for later.

Cash equivalents are holdings that behave almost like cash but earn a little return, such as money held in a money market fund. They are where an account often keeps money that is not currently invested.

Alternatives is a broad label for assets outside these common kinds, such as property funds or private investments. Most customers meet them rarely, so we treat them lightly.

Each of these instruments deserves its own study, and each will get it. For now we hold the simple picture. A wealth account holds instruments, and instruments come in these broad families.

## Holdings, Positions, and the Portfolio

Once a customer owns instruments, we need words for what they hold. When a customer owns a quantity of one instrument, we call that a position. A position in a share, for example, records how many shares are held and what was paid for them. The whole set of a customer's positions, taken together, is called their portfolio.

A portfolio can be looked at in useful ways. Its allocation is how it is spread across the different kinds of instrument: how much in equities, how much in fixed income, how much in cash, and so on. Allocation is one of the main things advice and planning try to shape.

There is one more idea here that we must introduce now, because it returns again and again. It is cost basis. Cost basis is the record of what was paid for a holding. It matters because tax and performance both depend on it. When a customer buys the same instrument more than once, at different prices, each separate purchase is recorded as its own lot, often called a tax lot, with its own cost and its own date. So a single position in one share may be made of several lots underneath, each bought at a different time and price. This detail seems small, but it will matter a great deal when we reach tax and performance. We introduce it here and study it later.

We can pause on one point from Chapter 1. How much a customer holds, the number of shares or units, is recorded inside the bank, in the account. That part of the truth is internal, and it is certain. What those holdings are worth is another matter entirely, and for that we must look outside.

## What It Is Worth: Market Data

We have said several times that the value of a holding comes from outside the bank. Here we meet the outside. The information about what instruments are worth, and at what price they can be bought and sold, is called market data. It is the part of wealth that most sets it apart from retail and lending, and it is worth looking at with care even in a survey.

The first thing to understand is that a single instrument does not have one simple price. At any moment there are several numbers to consider. There is the price of the last trade that happened, which is already in the past. There is the highest price a buyer is currently willing to pay, called the bid. There is the lowest price a seller is currently willing to accept, called the ask. These are not the same, and the true price to buy or sell sits somewhere among them. When we speak of an instrument's price, we must be clear about which of these we mean.

The second thing is that these numbers move constantly. While the market is open, prices can change many times a second. Wealth systems often receive this information as a stream that never stops during trading hours. A value shown on a screen may be correct for only a moment before it changes again.

Market data also comes at different speeds and qualities. Some feeds are live, showing prices as they happen. Others are delayed on purpose, showing prices as they were a short time ago. The choice between them affects both cost and accuracy, and it is a real decision in any wealth system.

Finally, the instruments themselves can change through events called corporate actions. A company may split its shares, so that one share becomes several. It may pay a dividend, a share of its profits, to those who hold it. Two companies may merge. Each of these changes what a holding is, or what it is worth, without the customer buying or selling anything. Corporate actions are a large topic on their own, and we will give them proper attention later.

This is the source of wealth's central difficulty, made concrete. The value is external, because the market sets it. It is live, because it never stops moving. And it is uncertain, because at any moment there is no single agreed price, only a set of numbers we must choose among. Everything else in wealth is shaped by this fact.

## How Ownership Changes: The Life of a Trade

In retail, money changed hands. In wealth, ownership of instruments changes hands, and money moves in the other direction to pay for it. The process by which this happens is the life of a trade, and it has more steps than a simple payment.

It begins with an order. An order is an instruction to buy or sell a certain instrument. Orders come in types. A market order asks to trade at once, at whatever the current price is. A limit order asks to trade only at a stated price or better. There are others, and each expresses a different wish about price and speed.

The order is then executed. To execute an order is to actually carry out the trade in the market, matching a buyer with a seller at an agreed price. Because the market is moving, the price at which an order executes may not be exactly the price the customer saw when they placed it. This gap between the expected price and the real one is a normal part of trading.

After execution comes settlement. Settlement is the moment when the exchange truly completes: the money passes to the seller, and the ownership of the instrument passes to the buyer. This does not happen instantly. It happens a set number of days after the trade. In the time between the trade and its settlement, the deal is agreed but not yet finished.

Around all of this sit custody and clearing. Custody is the safe holding of the instruments a customer owns, often by a specialised institution rather than by the customer directly. Clearing is the work that stands between trade and settlement, making sure both sides will meet their part of the deal. These are quiet, essential parts of wealth, and we will open them up in a later chapter.

We can already see how much more involved this is than a retail payment. A trade has two sides that must match, a price set by a moving market, a delay before it completes, and institutions that hold and guarantee it. Buying an instrument is not like paying a shop.

## How We Measure: Performance

Wealth raises a question that retail and lending never had to ask. How well is the customer doing? A retail balance simply is what it is. A loan follows its schedule. But an investment is meant to grow, and so we want to measure whether, and how much, it has grown. This measurement is called performance, and it is harder than it first appears.

The basic idea is the return: how much the value of a portfolio has risen or fallen over a period, as a percentage. The difficulty comes from money moving in and out. If a customer adds money to a portfolio, its value goes up, but not because the investments did well. If they take money out, its value falls, but not because the investments did badly. To measure the performance of the investments themselves, we must separate the effect of the customer's own deposits and withdrawals from the effect of the market.

Because of this, there is more than one kind of return, and they answer slightly different questions. One kind measures how the investments performed, setting aside the customer's deposits and withdrawals. Another measures the customer's own experience, including the effect of when they added and removed money. Both are useful, and they can give different numbers for the same portfolio. We will study the difference between them carefully later.

Two more ideas belong here. A benchmark is a standard we compare against, such as a broad market index, to judge whether a portfolio did well or badly relative to the market as a whole. Attribution is the work of explaining where a portfolio's return came from, breaking it down into its causes. Both help turn a single number into understanding. We name them now and return to them in their own chapter.

## What We Owe: Tax

Wealth carries a weight of tax that retail and lending largely spare the customer. The reason is simple. When an investment is sold for more than it cost, that gain is often taxable. When it is sold for less, that loss can often reduce tax. So nearly every sale has a tax consequence, and the details matter.

Cost basis, which we met earlier, is central to this. The gain or loss on a sale is the sale price minus the cost basis. When a holding is made of several lots bought at different prices, the question of which lot we are selling changes the gain, and so changes the tax. There are established methods for choosing, and the choice can be made deliberately to manage tax.

A few more ideas will return in the tax chapter. Tax-loss harvesting is the practice of deliberately selling holdings that have fallen, to realise a loss that offsets gains elsewhere and reduces tax. The wash sale rule is a limit on this. If the same holding is bought back too soon after selling it at a loss, the loss may not be counted. And at the end of the year, the customer receives tax documents, such as the form known in the United States as the 1099, that report their gains, losses, and income for the tax authority. Each of these deserves careful treatment, which we give it later.

## Getting Help: Advice and Planning

Many customers do not manage their wealth alone. They receive advice, either from a person or from a program, and this advice is itself a large part of the wealth domain.

Advice usually begins with goals. A customer has something they are saving toward, such as retirement or a home, and a plan is built to reach it. To support this, we make projections: estimates of how a portfolio might grow over many years, to see whether a goal can be reached. These projections are estimates, not promises, because the future of the market is unknown.

Advice also acts on the portfolio over time. Rebalancing is the act of bringing a portfolio's allocation back to its intended shape, after market movements have pulled it away. A model portfolio is a standard mix of investments, designed for a certain kind of customer, that many portfolios can be built to match.

One distinction here is important enough to name now. In some arrangements the adviser may act on their own judgment, buying and selling without asking each time. This is called discretionary. In others, the adviser may only act with the customer's approval for each decision. This is called non-discretionary. The difference decides who holds the power to act, and it carries real weight in the rules we turn to next. Automated advice, often called robo-advice, applies many of these same ideas through software rather than a person. We will explore advice and planning fully in their own chapter.

## The Rules: Regulation

All three categories of banking answer to rules, but wealth is watched especially closely. The reason is that a customer's savings are at risk, and the choices made can be complex and hard to judge. Regulation in wealth exists mainly to protect the customer.

A central idea is that advice must be appropriate for the customer. An older idea, called suitability, asked whether a recommendation was suitable given the customer's situation. Newer standards ask for more. In the United States, a standard called Regulation Best Interest, often shortened to Reg BI, requires that recommendations be in the customer's best interest. A still stronger duty, called a fiduciary duty, requires an adviser to put the customer's interest ahead of their own.

Other rules run alongside these. Know Your Customer, or KYC, is the requirement to establish who the customer really is and to understand their situation. We met a lighter form of this in retail, but in wealth it reaches further, into the customer's goals, their means, and how much risk they are willing to take. Disclosure is the duty to tell the customer clearly about risks, costs, and any conflicts of interest before they act. Together, these rules shape much of what a wealth system must record and prove. We give them a chapter of their own later.

## Seeing It All: Reporting and Aggregation

The last part of our survey is how the whole picture is gathered and shown. A customer, and the bank, need to see clearly what is held, what it is worth, how it has performed, and what is owed. This gathering and showing is a domain of its own.

The most familiar form is the statement: a periodic summary of an account, listing its holdings, their values, and the activity over the period. Alongside statements come the tax documents we mentioned, produced once a year to report gains, losses, and income.

A newer and growing need is aggregation. Many customers hold accounts at more than one institution. Accounts held elsewhere are often called held-away, or external, accounts. Customers increasingly want to see all of it in one place, across institutions, in a single unified view. This is difficult, because the data comes from many sources in many forms, and it must be brought together into one clear picture.

This last aspect points back to the whole map we drew in Part 1. A unified view of a customer's finances may bring together their everyday accounts, their loans, and their investments, all on one screen. The categories we separated for the sake of understanding are, in the customer's eyes, one financial life. Bringing them back together is one of the harder and more interesting problems in the field, and we will look at it in its place.

## The Road Into Part 2

We have now seen the whole of wealth. We have seen where it is held, what is owned, what we hold as a result, and what it is worth. We have seen how ownership changes, how we measure success, what we owe, how advice fits in, what rules apply, and how it is all reported. That is a great deal, and we have touched each part only lightly.

Looking at the whole, we can see why wealth is the hardest of the three categories, and why it earns the rest of this book. Part of the reason is the one we have carried since Chapter 1. Its truth is external, live, and uncertain. But we can now add a second reason. Wealth has a large number of parts, and those parts interact. Cost basis reaches into tax and into performance. The type of account changes the tax and the rules. Market data touches everything that involves a value. Advice is bound to regulation. No part stands alone. This web of connections, laid over a truth that never holds still, is what makes wealth so demanding to model well.

We should also remember what we said at the end of the last two chapters. Wealth does not start from nothing. It rests on the foundation that retail and lending built. It needs to know its customer, which retail began and regulation here extends. It needs an account to hold what is owned. It needs to move money in and out, along the rails retail established, to pay for what is bought and to receive what is sold. Onto that foundation, wealth adds everything we have just surveyed.

Part 1 is now complete in all but one chapter. Before we begin the deep study of wealth, there is a small piece of unfinished work. Along the way we met products that did not sit cleanly in one category: the credit card that both pays and borrows, and others like it. In the next chapter, the last of Part 1, we look at these crossing products directly. They will test the map we have drawn, and they will lead us, at last, to the edge of wealth, where the rest of the book begins.
