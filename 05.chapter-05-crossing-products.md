# Chapter 5: Products That Cross Categories

At the end of the last chapter we said there was a small piece of unfinished work before we begin our deep study of wealth. This is that work. Along the way, in the earlier chapters, we kept meeting products that did not sit neatly inside one category. The clearest was the credit card, which pays a shop like a retail card but borrows money like a loan. There were others. In this final chapter of Part 1, we look at these crossing products directly.

We have two reasons for doing so. The first is that they test the map we have drawn. A product that belongs to two categories at once might seem to break a map built on three separate categories. We will see that it does not. In fact, it shows why we built the map the way we did. The second reason is that the most interesting of these products sit at the edge of wealth. By walking through them in order, we move from the familiar ground of retail and lending toward wealth, and we arrive, at the end, ready to begin.

## A Product Is a Package

To make sense of crossing products, we return to an idea from Chapter 1. We built our map around operations, not products. A product is a package. It is how a bank presents something to a customer. An operation is what actually happens inside that package. We said then that a single product can hold more than one operation at once. Now we meet the products that do.

This is the idea that explains every example in this chapter. When a product seems to belong to two categories, we do not need to force it into one. We simply describe it as what it is: a package that composes two operations. Each operation still belongs cleanly to its category. The product is a combination, but the operations underneath are not confused. Had we built our map around products, these cases would be awkward exceptions. Because we built it around operations, they are simple to explain. Every crossing product in this chapter is really two operations, under one name.

We will see three kinds of crossing. First, products where retail meets lending. Then products where wealth meets lending. Finally, a product where retail meets wealth. The order is deliberate. It carries us, step by step, toward the category the rest of this book is about.

## Where Retail Meets Lending

The first crossings are the ones most of us know from daily life. They join retail, the holding and moving of money, with lending, the borrowing of money over time.

The clearest example is the credit card. When a credit card pays a shop, it behaves exactly like a retail payment. It uses the card network, one of the rails we met in Chapter 2, and the money reaches the shop in the same way a debit card's would. In that moment, the two cards are hard to tell apart. But the source of the money is completely different. A debit card draws money the customer already has, from their account. A credit card borrows the money, drawing on a revolving credit line of the kind we met in Chapter 3. So a single tap of a credit card composes two operations at once. There is a retail payment, which moves money to the shop. And there is a lending operation, which adds to a debt the customer will repay later, with interest if they carry it. One action, two operations, each in its own category.

A second example is the overdraft. A checking account is a retail product, and normally it cannot fall below zero. An overdraft arrangement changes that. It lets the account go negative when a payment is larger than the balance, and the bank covers the difference. But that negative balance is not really part of the account. It is a small, short loan. The bank has lent the customer the shortfall, and it will usually charge a fee or interest for doing so. So an overdraft is a retail account with a small lending facility attached. When the balance is positive, it is pure retail. The moment it goes negative, a lending operation quietly begins.

## Where Wealth Meets Lending

The next crossings bring us to the edge of wealth. Here lending reaches into the wealth account, using the customer's investments as its security. These products are less familiar than a credit card, so we introduce them plainly and leave their full detail for Part 2.

The first is the margin loan. In a wealth account, a customer can borrow money from the broker to buy more investments than their own cash would allow. Borrowing in this way is called buying on margin, and the loan itself is a margin loan. This is a lending operation, and it is secured, as many loans are. What is unusual is the security. The collateral is the customer's own investments. The portfolio backs the loan.

This is where wealth's special nature begins to show. In ordinary secured lending, the collateral is something whose value moves slowly and steadily, such as a house or a car. In a margin loan, the collateral is investments, whose value moves on its own, all the time, and cannot be known in advance. This is exactly the property we named for wealth in Chapter 1. Because of it, a margin loan carries a risk that an ordinary loan does not. If the value of the investments falls too far, the broker may demand more collateral or sell some of the holdings to protect itself. This demand is called a margin call. We name this danger here and study it properly later. For now, the point is the composition. A margin loan is a wealth holding and a lending operation joined together, where the wealth holding serves as the security for the loan.

The second is closely related. It is a line of credit secured by investments, often called a securities-based line of credit. Like a margin loan, it lets the customer borrow against their portfolio, which serves as collateral. The difference is what the money is for. A margin loan is used to buy more investments. A securities-based line of credit is used for ordinary spending, outside the portfolio, while the investments stay in place. It lets a customer get cash without selling their holdings. This has an advantage we can already appreciate from Chapter 4. Selling investments can create a tax bill, and borrowing against them instead avoids that sale. So this product joins a lending operation to a wealth holding, in order to get cash while leaving the investments in place.

## Where Retail Meets Wealth

The last crossing runs in the other direction. In the products above, lending reached into wealth. Here retail reaches into wealth, so that a wealth account takes on the everyday role of a retail one.

A wealth account almost always holds some cash that is not currently invested, waiting to be used. Left alone, that cash earns nothing. A sweep is an arrangement that automatically moves this idle cash into a holding that earns a small return, such as a money market fund, and moves it back when it is needed. The customer does nothing; the account manages the cash on its own. In this small way, a wealth account begins to behave like a retail one, looking after everyday money.

Some accounts go much further. A cash management account is a wealth account that also offers the features of everyday banking: a debit card, bill payments, and a salary paid straight in. The customer can invest and, from the same account, spend and pay bills as they would from a checking account. Here the two categories are fully joined. The account holds investments, which is wealth, and it moves everyday money, which is retail, in one place.

This last crossing points ahead to something we raised at the end of Chapter 4. In the customer's eyes, their money is one thing, not three. A product that lets them invest and spend from a single account is a small step toward that single, joined view of a financial life. We separated the categories to understand them. Products like this one begin to put them back together.

## Why the Map Still Holds, and What Comes Next

We have now seen products that cross every pair of categories: retail with lending, wealth with lending, and retail with wealth. None of them broke our map. Each turned out to be a package that composes two operations, and each operation stayed cleanly in its own category. This is the reward of building the map around operations rather than products. The crossing products, which could have been confusing, became simple to describe. We did not need special cases. We needed only to name the operations inside each package.

We can also see, looking back over this chapter, that the crossings which touch wealth already carry wealth's distinctive nature into them. The margin loan and the securities-based line of credit are risky in a way ordinary loans are not, because their collateral is investments whose value never stops moving. The cash management account joins everyday money to investments whose worth is set outside the bank. Even when wealth is only one half of a product, its external, live, and uncertain truth shapes the whole. This shows us, from the outside, a little of what makes wealth demanding, before we step inside.

With this, Part 1 is complete. We set out to build a map of the banking domain, and we have it. We divided the domain into three categories by the operation each performs. We looked closely at retail and at lending, and we surveyed the whole of wealth. We examined the products that cross between categories, and we found that our map held. Along the way, one theme returned again and again: of the three categories, wealth is the hardest, because its truth lives outside the bank, moves on its own, and can never be fully known.

That theme now becomes our subject. From here, we leave retail and lending behind, and we turn to wealth alone. In Part 2, we begin the deep study that the survey in Chapter 4 laid out for us. We take the parts of wealth one at a time, and for each we do what this book has done throughout: we first say plainly what it is, and then we look as closely as it deserves.
