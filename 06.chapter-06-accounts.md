# Part 2: Inside Wealth

Part 1 gave us the map. We now leave retail and lending behind and enter the one category this book was written for. In Part 2 we study wealth in depth, one part at a time. We follow the order of the survey in Chapter 4. We begin, as we did with retail, at the account. But this time we do not pass over it quickly. # Chapter 6: Accounts

Every part of wealth we are about to study lives inside an account. Instruments are held in it. Trades happen within it. Performance is measured on it. Tax is reported for it. So the account is the natural place to begin, because it is the container for all the rest.

In Chapter 4 we said only a little about accounts. We noted that a wealth account holds assets rather than a simple balance, and that its type shapes tax, limits, and who decides. Now we open it up. We look at what an account really holds, and why its value behaves so differently from a retail balance. We look at who owns an account, and at how the type of account changes what it is. We follow an account from the moment it opens. And we end by placing the account exactly where it belongs in the story of this book: at the meeting point between the truth the bank holds and the truth the market decides.

## What a Wealth Account Holds

### Positions, Cash, and the Shape of an Account

A retail account, we said in Chapter 2, holds a balance: a single amount of money. A wealth account is not like this. It does not hold one number. It holds a collection of things.

The first of these is its positions. A position is a holding of one instrument. It records which instrument is held, and how much of it. A wealth account may hold many positions at once: some shares of one company, some units of a fund, some bonds, and so on. We will study positions closely in a later chapter. For now we only need to know that the account contains a set of them.

The second is cash. Almost every wealth account holds some cash that is not currently invested. It may be money the customer has added but not yet used, or money that has come in from a sale or a dividend and is waiting to be reinvested. We met in Chapter 5 how this cash is often swept into a holding that earns a small return. Either way, the account holds a cash amount alongside its positions.

There may also be activity in progress: a trade that has been made but not yet settled, or a transfer that has not yet arrived. These are temporary, but the account must keep track of them.

We can sketch the shape of a wealth account.

```
Account
  accountId      a unique name for this account
  owner          who the account belongs to
  registration   the form of ownership, such as individual or joint
  type           brokerage, retirement, or managed
  taxTreatment   taxable or tax-advantaged
  cash           uninvested cash held in the account
  positions      the set of holdings, each a position
  status         open, restricted, or closed
```

As before, this is not code to run. It is a plain description of what an account holds. The important thing to see is that positions is a set, not a single value. The account is a container. Most of what makes wealth rich and difficult comes from what is inside that container, and from one fact about it that we turn to now.

### A Value That Is Not Stored

A customer naturally wants to know one number above all: what is my account worth? For a retail account, this is simple. The balance is the value, and the bank stores it. For a wealth account, it is not simple at all.

The value of a wealth account is not a number the bank keeps. It is a number the bank works out. To find it, we take the cash in the account and add the value of every position. But the value of a position is the quantity held multiplied by the current price of the instrument. And the current price, as we learned in Chapter 4, comes from outside the bank, from the market, and it never stops moving.

This has a consequence worth stating plainly. The value of a wealth account can change when nothing in the account has changed. The customer buys nothing and sells nothing. No cash comes in or out. The positions are exactly as they were a minute ago. And yet the account is worth a different amount now, because the market has moved the prices of what it holds. A retail balance never behaved this way. It changed only when the bank recorded a movement. A wealth account's value changes on its own, all day long, driven by prices the bank does not control.

Here we can see, in the plainest terms, the seam at the center of this whole book. What the account holds is internal and certain. The number of shares, the units of each fund, the cash amount: these are recorded inside the bank, and they are exact. What the account is worth is external and uncertain. It depends on prices set outside, which move constantly and can never be known exactly. The account is where these two kinds of truth meet. Everything else in wealth is built on top of this meeting.

## Who Owns the Account

Before we look at the different types of account, we must ask a simpler question: who owns it? This is not a small matter. The form of ownership decides who may act on the account, who is responsible for its tax, and what happens to it in certain events.

An account may be owned by a single person. This is the simplest form, and the most common. It may instead be owned jointly, by two people together, such as a couple, with rules about how each may act. An adult may hold an account on behalf of a child, to be handed over when the child grows up. An account may be owned by a trust, or by a company or other organisation rather than by a person at all.

This form of ownership is often called the account's registration, or its titling. It carries real consequences. In a jointly owned account, either owner may usually trade, and the tax may be shared. In an account held for a child, the adult acts but the account is meant for the child. In a trust or a company account, the rules follow the trust or the company, not an individual.

We can also see here a link back to a rule we met in Chapter 4. Before an account is opened, the bank must know who the customer is. This is the Know Your Customer duty. The form of ownership is part of that knowledge. The bank must understand not only that an account exists, but exactly whose it is, and on whose behalf it is held.

## The Account Type Shapes Everything

We come now to the most important thing about a wealth account: its type. In Chapter 4 we named three broad kinds, brokerage, retirement, and managed. Here we see why the type is not a label but a deep property. The type decides how the account is taxed, what may be put into it and taken out, and who makes its decisions. Two accounts holding the very same investments can behave completely differently, simply because they are of different types.

Behind the many named types lies one master distinction. Some accounts are taxable. Others are tax-advantaged. Almost everything else follows from which of these an account is.

### The Taxable Account

The taxable account is the plainest kind, and the brokerage account is its common form. It works in a direct way. The customer puts money in, buys and sells investments, and takes money out, with few special limits. There is no cap on how much may be added. Money can be withdrawn at any time.

The price of this freedom is tax, paid as things happen. When an investment is sold for a gain, that gain is generally taxable in the year of the sale. When an investment pays a dividend or interest, that income is generally taxable in the year it is received. We will study the mechanics of this in the tax chapter. For now, the point is simple. A taxable account is free in its movement, but its gains and income are taxed as they occur.

Because it is so unrestricted, the taxable account is the baseline against which the others are understood. The other types offer tax advantages, and in return they impose rules. To see those rules clearly, it helps to remember what the taxable account does not have: no cap on what goes in, no limit on when money comes out, and no special tax treatment. Everything the next accounts add is a change to one of these.

### Tax-Advantaged Accounts

A tax-advantaged account offers a better tax outcome, in exchange for accepting rules. The retirement account is the main example, built to encourage people to save for later life. There are several kinds, but they share a common design, and two ideas explain most of them.

The first idea is when the tax is paid. Tax-advantaged accounts follow one of two models. In the first, money goes in before tax. The amount put in may lower the customer's taxable income now, and the investments grow without being taxed year to year. The tax is paid later, when money is finally taken out, and is treated then as income. This model moves the tax from now to later. In the second model, money goes in after tax. There is no benefit now, but the investments grow, and later the money is taken out without further tax. A common form of this second model is called a Roth account. Here the customer pays tax now and owes none when the money is taken out. Which model an account follows is a basic fact about it, and a wealth system must always know which it is.

The second idea is the set of rules that come in return for the advantage. Because these accounts are subsidised through tax, they come with limits. There is usually a cap on how much may be added each year, set by law, and the system must track a customer's additions against that cap. There are usually rules about taking money out. Money removed too early, before a set retirement age, may face a penalty on top of the normal tax. Some kinds of account also require that withdrawals begin once the customer reaches a certain age, whether they want the money or not. These rules are driven by time and age, and enforcing them correctly is part of what a system for these accounts must do.

Two further points are worth naming. Some of these accounts are arranged through an employer rather than opened by a person alone. In an employer arrangement, money is often taken straight from pay, and the employer may add a contribution of its own. And when a person leaves a job, or wishes to move money from one such account to another, they may do so through a rollover. A rollover moves money between tax-advantaged accounts without breaking their tax treatment, provided it is done in the correct way. A wealth system must handle a rollover carefully, because a mistake can turn a protected movement into a taxable one.

We are not, in this chapter, studying how the tax itself is calculated. That belongs to the tax chapter. Here we are learning what makes a tax-advantaged account a distinct kind of container: a special tax model, a cap on what goes in, and rules on what comes out.

### Managed Accounts

The third kind of account differs along a completely different line. The taxable and tax-advantaged accounts differ in how they are taxed. A managed account differs in who makes its decisions.

In the accounts we have described so far, the customer decides. They choose what to buy and when to sell. Such an account is called self-directed. In a managed account, the customer hands some or all of these decisions to someone else: a professional manager, or a program.

Here a distinction from Chapter 4 returns, and it matters greatly. In some managed arrangements, the manager may act on their own judgment, buying and selling without asking the customer each time. This is called discretionary. In others, the manager may only act with the customer's approval for each decision. This is called non-discretionary. The difference decides who holds the power to act, and it has great importance in the rules we will study later.

Managed accounts come in several forms. In one common form, called a separately managed account, the customer owns the investments directly, but a professional runs the account for them. In another, called a wrap account, the services of managing, trading, and advising are bundled together and paid for by a single fee. Increasingly, the management is done by software rather than a person, in the automated services often called robo-advice.

One feature of managed accounts is worth noting now, because it is something a system must track. A managed account usually charges a fee based on the value of the assets it holds, taken as a small percentage over time. This fee is not a single charge. It grows with the account and is collected regularly, and the system must calculate and apply it. We will return to management and advice in their own chapter. Here we need only see that a managed account is defined not by its tax, but by the handing over of decisions, and by the fee that this service carries.

## Opening and Funding an Account

An account must be created before it can hold anything, and money and investments must reach it. This is a shorter story than the life of a loan, but it has parts worth knowing.

An account begins by being opened. The customer applies, and the bank must satisfy the Know Your Customer duty we have mentioned: it must establish who the customer is and understand their situation. The form of ownership is settled, the type of account is chosen, and any agreements are signed. Only then is the account ready.

An empty account is of little use, so it must be funded. Money and investments can reach an account in a few ways. Cash can be moved in from a bank account, along the same retail rails we studied in Chapter 2. Investments can be transferred in from another institution, so that holdings already owned elsewhere are moved into this account without being sold. And for a retirement account, money may arrive through the rollover we described, carried over from another tax-advantaged account. Each of these routes fills the account in a different way, and each must preserve the right records, especially the record of what was paid for each holding, which we will need later.

Once open and funded, an account has a status. Most of the time it is simply open and active. At times it may be restricted, so that certain actions are not allowed, perhaps for a compliance reason or while a problem is resolved. In the end it may be closed. The status is a small but real part of the account's state, and the system must respect it before allowing any action.

## The Account as the Meeting Point

We began Part 2 with the account because it is the container for everything else. We can now see that it is more than a container. It is the place where the two truths of wealth come together.

Inside the account sit the holdings and the cash. These are the internal truth. The bank records them, and they are exact. The account knows, without any doubt, how many shares and how many units it holds, and how much cash sits beside them. This part of wealth is as certain as a retail balance.

But the worth of those holdings is the external truth. It is set by the market, outside the bank, and it moves without pause. The account's value is not stored; it is worked out, again and again, from prices the bank receives but does not choose. This is why a wealth account is so unlike a retail one, even though both are, in the end, accounts owned by a customer.

The account also shows us, once more, how wealth builds on what came before. It is still an account with an owner, as retail taught us. It still holds cash and moves money in and out along retail's rails. Onto that familiar base, wealth adds its positions, its many types, and its rules of tax and control. The account is retail's idea, extended until it can carry all of wealth.

With the container understood, we can ask what goes inside it. In the next chapter we study instruments: the shares, funds, bonds, and other assets that fill a wealth account. We have seen the container. Now we turn to what fills it.
