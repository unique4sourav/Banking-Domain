# Chapter 7: Instruments

In the last chapter we saw the account, the container that holds a customer's wealth. We said an account holds positions, and that a position is a holding of one instrument. Now we turn to the instruments themselves. These are the things a customer owns: the shares, the funds, the bonds, and the rest. If the account is the container, instruments are what fill it.

There is an idea to settle before we look at the kinds of instrument, because it shapes how we think about all of them. An instrument is not the same as owning it. The instrument is the thing itself, defined once and shared by everyone. A share in a certain company is one instrument, whether a thousand customers hold it or none do. What each customer has is a position: their own holding of that shared instrument. In this chapter we study the instruments, the shared definitions. In the next, we study positions, the individual holdings. Keeping these two apart will make both clearer.

We will also carry one question through the whole chapter. For each kind of instrument, we will ask how its value is known. This turns out to be the thread that connects them. Some instruments have a price that moves every second. Some have a price set once a day. Some are hard to value at all. The differences matter, and they lead us, at the chapter's end, toward the study of market data that follows.

## What an Instrument Is

An instrument is a thing that can be owned and traded. That is a broad idea, and it needs to be made precise. An instrument has an identity, a description, and a way of being priced. It exists as a definition, held once and shared by all who deal in it.

This makes an instrument a piece of what we can call reference data. Reference data is information that is shared and looked up, rather than owned by one customer. The definition of a share, a bond, or a fund does not belong to any single account. It belongs to the market as a whole, and every account that holds it points to the same definition. A bank keeps a central store of these definitions, so that every part of its systems agrees on what each instrument is. This store is often called a security master, because a security is another common word for an instrument.

We can sketch the shape of an instrument's definition.

```
Instrument
  instrumentId   a stable unique identifier for this instrument
  symbol         a short readable code, such as a ticker
  name           the readable name of the instrument
  assetClass     the kind: equity, fund, bond, option, and so on
  market         where the instrument trades
  currency       the currency in which its price is quoted
```

As before, this is a plain description of what the domain holds, not code to run. This is the shared part, common to every kind of instrument. But different kinds carry different extra facts. A bond must record its interest and its maturity. An option must record what it is based on and when it expires. A fund must record what it holds. So the definition above is only the common core. Each asset class adds fields of its own, and we will see some of these as we go.

The separation between the instrument and the position is worth keeping in mind. The instrument says what a thing is. The position says how much of it a particular account holds. When a price changes, it changes for the instrument, and every position in it is affected at once. This is why we treat the instrument as a shared definition, and the position as a private holding that refers to it.

## Naming an Instrument

Before an instrument can be held, priced, or traded, it must be named without confusion. This sounds simple. It is not. Naming instruments correctly is one of the quiet, hard problems of a wealth system, and getting it wrong causes real harm.

The most familiar name is the ticker symbol: a short code, such as a few letters, used to refer to a share on an exchange. Tickers are made for people. They are easy to read and easy to type. But they are not reliable as a true identity. The same ticker can mean different things on different exchanges. A ticker can be given up by one company and later used by another. A company can change its ticker. So a ticker is a helpful label, but it is not a safe way to tell one instrument from another for certain.

For that, the industry uses longer, stricter identifiers. These are codes designed to point to exactly one instrument and never to be reused or confused. Different systems of them exist, known by short names such as CUSIP and ISIN, each covering certain markets. What matters to us is not their detail but their purpose. They give each instrument a stable, unique identity that does not change and does not clash.

The reason this matters is direct. If a system mistakes one instrument for another, everything built on top of that mistake is wrong. The wrong price is shown. The wrong trade is made. The wrong holding is valued. Because so much depends on it, a wealth system treats the identity of an instrument with great care. It trusts the stable identifiers for truth, and uses the friendly ticker only as a label for people to read. This is why the security master exists: to hold one correct, agreed definition of each instrument, with its true identity, that all other systems can rely on.

## The Families of Instruments

With the idea of an instrument in hand, we can meet its main families. Each is a broad kind of thing to own, with its own nature. For each, we say what it is, note the facts that define it, and see how it behaves. We move roughly from the simplest to the most complex.

### Equities

An equity is a share in a company. To hold one is to own a small part of that company, with a claim on its assets and a share in its success or failure. Equities are also called shares or stocks. They are, for most customers, the most familiar kind of investment.

An equity's definition is simple. It names the company, the exchange where it trades, and the currency of its price. It does not carry the extra machinery that other instruments need. There is no maturity, no interest, no expiry. A share simply exists, and is held, for as long as the customer wants it and the company lasts.

The behaviour of an equity is also direct. Its price moves continuously while its market is open, driven by the fortunes of the company and the judgment of the market. It may pay a share of the company's profits, called a dividend, to those who hold it. And it can be affected by the corporate actions we met in Chapter 4, such as a split that turns one share into several. There is a further small distinction, between ordinary shares and a kind called preferred shares, which stand closer to a bond in how they pay. We note it and move on.

For our purposes, the equity is the baseline instrument. It is identified simply, priced continuously, and held without complication. The instruments that follow are, in various ways, more involved than this.

### Funds

A fund is a way to own many things at once. Instead of buying a long list of investments one by one, a customer buys a single fund, and the fund holds a wide collection of underlying assets. Owning a unit of the fund means owning a small share of everything the fund holds. The great value of a fund is spread: with one holding, a customer owns a little of many things, which lowers the risk that any single one will hurt them. This spreading of risk is called diversification.

Funds come in two main forms, and the difference between them is important, because they are priced and traded in different ways.

The first is the mutual fund. A mutual fund is priced once a day. After the market closes, the fund adds up the value of everything it holds, divides by the number of units, and arrives at a single value per unit for that day. This value is called the net asset value. A customer who buys or sells a mutual fund does so at that day's net asset value, not at a price that moves through the day. So a mutual fund has no live price. It has one price per day.

The second is the exchange-traded fund, usually shortened to ETF. An ETF also holds a collection of assets, but it trades on an exchange throughout the day, like a share. It has a live, moving price, set by buying and selling. It still has a net asset value underneath, based on its holdings, but its trading price can drift a little above or below that value. So an ETF behaves, for trading, much like an equity, while being, in structure, a fund.

Funds usually charge a fee for their management, taken as a small yearly percentage of the amount held. For a wealth system, the key facts about a fund are its form, which decides how it is priced, and, when needed, the collection of things it holds underneath, which matters when we later add up what a customer truly owns across everything.

### Fixed Income

Fixed income is the family of bonds and instruments like them. We have met the idea already. In Chapter 3, on lending, we said that a bond is, in essence, a loan made tradable. Here we see it from the owner's side. When a customer holds a bond, they have lent money to the body that issued it, a government or a company, and in return they are owed regular interest and, at the end, the return of the original sum.

This makes a bond far richer in detail than a share. A bond's definition must record several facts that a share never needs.

```
Bond
  issuer          who borrowed the money
  faceValue       the sum to be returned at the end
  couponRate      the rate of interest paid
  couponFrequency how often the interest is paid
  maturity        the date the face value is returned
```

Each of these shapes how the bond behaves. The interest, called the coupon, is paid on a regular schedule until the bond matures. At maturity, the face value is returned and the bond ends. This is the same shape of a schedule we saw with a loan, now attached to something a customer can own and sell.

Two features of bonds are worth naming, because they surprise those who meet them first. The first is how a bond is priced. A bond's price is usually given as a percentage of its face value, so a price of ninety-eight means ninety-eight percent of the sum to be returned. Tied to this price is the bond's yield, the true return it offers, and price and yield move in opposite directions: when the price rises, the yield falls, and when the price falls, the yield rises. The second feature connects straight back to lending. Because a bond pays interest on a schedule but can be bought or sold on any day, interest builds up between payments, just as it did on a loan. When a bond is bought part way through a period, the buyer pays the seller for the interest that has built up since the last payment. This built-up interest is called accrued interest, and it is the same idea of accrual we studied in Chapter 3, now appearing inside an instrument.

Bonds come in many kinds, from those issued by governments to those issued by companies, and they carry a judgment of how likely the interest and return are to be paid, called a credit rating. We do not need every detail. The point is that fixed income is an attribute-rich family, carrying interest, a schedule, and a maturity, and behaving in ways that a simple share does not.

### Options and Derivatives

Some instruments do not have a value of their own. Instead, their value comes from something else. These are called derivatives, because their worth is derived from another instrument, known as the underlying. The most common derivative a customer might meet is the option.

An option is a contract. It gives its holder a right, but not a duty, to buy or to sell a certain underlying instrument, at a set price, by a set date. An option to buy is called a call. An option to sell is called a put. The set price is called the strike, and the set date is called the expiry. So an option's definition records what it is based on, whether it is a call or a put, its strike, and its expiry.

An option behaves very differently from a share or a bond. Its value depends on the price of the underlying, on how much time is left before it expires, and on how sharply the underlying's price tends to move. It has a limited life: when the expiry date passes, the option is either used or it becomes worthless. This means an option must be watched as its expiry approaches, and handled at that expiry, in a way that a share, which simply persists, does not require.

Options and other derivatives are advanced, and they carry more risk than the instruments before them. We introduce them here so that they have a place on our map. Their full study is a large subject, and it belongs beyond the scope of this book. What we need to carry forward is their nature: an instrument whose value comes from another, and whose life ends on a fixed date.

### Cash Equivalents

Not everything in a wealth account is invested for growth. Some of it is held as something close to cash. Cash equivalents are holdings that behave almost like money but earn a small return, and that can be turned back into cash quickly and safely. A common form is the money market fund, which holds very short-term, very safe assets.

Cash equivalents are where an account keeps money that is waiting: money added but not yet invested, or money from a sale not yet put to use. We met this in Chapter 5, where idle cash is swept into such a holding to earn a little rather than nothing. For our purposes, cash equivalents are simple. Their value is stable, their risk is low, and they exist to hold value safely and briefly, ready for use.

### Alternatives

The last family is the broadest and the least tidy. Alternatives is a label for the investments that do not fit the families above. It covers such things as property funds, shares in private companies not traded on an exchange, funds that follow unusual strategies, holdings in physical goods, and newer kinds of digital asset.

Two things tend to set alternatives apart, and both make them harder to handle. The first is that they are often difficult to sell quickly. A share can be sold in seconds; a stake in a private company may take months to sell, if it can be sold at all. This quality of being easy or hard to sell is called liquidity, and alternatives are often described as illiquid. The second is that they are often hard to value. A share has a market price at every moment; an alternative may have no market price at all, and may be valued only now and then, by estimate. This makes alternatives difficult in a way that is worth pausing on. A volatile share is hard because its value moves too much. An alternative is hard because its value can barely be seen. Most ordinary customers meet alternatives rarely, so we treat them lightly, but we note that they touch the very thing this book keeps returning to: the question of what a holding is truly worth.

## Where an Instrument's Value Comes From

We promised to carry one question through this chapter: for each instrument, how is its value known? Having met the families, we can now draw them together, and in doing so we return to the idea at the center of this book.

The families differ sharply in how their value reaches us. An equity and an ETF have a live price that moves every second the market is open. A mutual fund has a single price a day, set after the market closes. A bond has a price, but tied to a yield and adjusted for interest that builds up between payments. An option has no simple price of its own; its value is worked out from the price of something else, from the time left, and from how much that something else tends to move. A cash equivalent barely moves at all. And an alternative may have no market price at all, and must be valued by estimate, now and then.

Behind this variety lies a single truth we first stated in Chapter 1. The value of what a customer owns comes from outside the bank. But we can now see that it comes from outside in different ways, and with different degrees of clearness and speed. For some instruments the outside speaks constantly and plainly. For others it speaks once a day, or through a calculation, or barely at all. The external truth is not one thing. It is a spectrum, from the second-by-second price of a share to the occasional estimate of a private holding.

This is why the next parts of our study follow as they do. Because value is external and comes in these many forms, we must look closely at how it is obtained and handled. That is the work of the chapter on market data. But before we can value what a customer holds, we must be able to describe what they hold in the first place, holding by holding. That is the work of the very next chapter. We have now seen what can be owned: the instruments, the shared definitions. In the next chapter we study what it means to own them: the position, where a single account meets a single instrument, and where the true record of a customer's wealth begins to take shape.
