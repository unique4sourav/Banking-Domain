# Chapter 8: Positions and the Portfolio

At the end of the last chapter, we said the position is where a single account meets a single instrument, and where the true record of a customer's wealth begins to take shape. This chapter is about that record. We have seen the container, the account. We have seen what fills it, the instruments. Now we look at the holdings themselves: what it means to own a quantity of an instrument, and what a system must remember about it.

The position seems, at first, like the simplest idea yet. It is just an amount of something owned. But it is not simple, and the reason is one word: cost. A position must remember not only what is held and how much, but what was paid for it. That single extra fact leads to almost everything that makes wealth demanding to record. It is the key to tax. It is the key to measuring performance. And it is where the two truths of this book, the certain and the uncertain, meet most closely and most personally. A position holds a quantity we know exactly and a value we cannot know exactly, and the tension between them is the story of this chapter. We begin with the single holding, and build up to the whole portfolio and to the overall shape, its allocation, that a customer often sees first of all.

## What a Position Is

A position is an account's holding of one instrument. If an account holds shares in a company, units of a fund, and some bonds, then it has three positions: one in each. A position joins the two things we have already studied. It points to an instrument, the shared definition from the last chapter, and it belongs to an account, the container from the chapter before. The position is where they meet.

At its plainest, a position records two things: which instrument is held, and how much of it. The quantity might be a number of shares, a number of units, or a number of bonds. This quantity is a fact the bank knows exactly. It is written in the bank's own records, and there is no doubt about it. Here we meet again the internal, certain side of wealth from Chapter 1. How much a customer holds is known for sure.

The value of the position is another matter. To find what a position is worth, we multiply its quantity by the current price of the instrument. The quantity is certain, but the price, as we now know well, comes from outside the bank and never stops moving. So the value of a position is uncertain and always changing, even though the quantity behind it is fixed. A position is a certain quantity with an uncertain value.

If that were the whole of it, a position would still be a simple record: an instrument, a quantity, and a value worked out from the price. But we have left out the one fact that makes positions rich, and we turn to it now.

## The Cost of a Holding

The missing fact is cost. When a customer buys an instrument, they pay for it, and that amount must be remembered. It is called the cost basis: the record of what was paid for a holding. Sometimes it includes more than the plain price, such as a fee paid to make the purchase, but it is, in essence, what the holding cost.

Why does this one fact matter so much? Because almost every important question about a holding is answered by comparing its value now with what it cost. The gain or loss on a holding is its current value minus its cost basis. If shares were bought for one thousand and are now worth one thousand four hundred, there is a gain of four hundred. That single comparison drives two of the largest topics still ahead of us. It drives tax, because a gain, once made real, is usually taxable. And it drives performance, because how much a holding has grown is measured against what was paid for it. Without the cost basis, neither question can be answered. This is why a wealth system guards the record of cost so carefully, and why, as we saw when an account is funded, that record must be carried along whenever holdings move from place to place.

So a position must remember cost. But remembering cost turns out to be harder than remembering a single number, because a holding is rarely bought all at once.

## A Position Is Made of Lots

A customer often buys the same instrument more than once. They may buy some shares of a company one month, and more of the same company a few months later, at a different price. Each of these purchases is its own event, with its own price and its own date. To keep the record of cost honest, we must remember each purchase separately. Each separate purchase is called a lot, or a tax lot.

This changes how we picture a position. A position is not a single quantity with a single cost. It is a collection of lots, each bought at its own price on its own day. The position is the sum of its lots. We can show this shape.

```
Position
  instrument     which instrument this holding is in
  lots           the separate purchases that make up the holding

Lot
  quantity       how many units this purchase was
  costPerUnit    the price paid for each unit
  acquiredOn     the date this purchase was made
```

As before, this describes what the domain holds, not code to run. Suppose a customer buys one hundred shares of a company at fifty, then later buys another hundred at sixty. Their position in that company is two hundred shares. But underneath, it is two lots: one hundred shares that cost fifty each, bought on the first date, and one hundred shares that cost sixty each, bought on the second. The position, seen from above, is two hundred shares. The position, seen in detail, is two lots with two different costs and two different dates.

A system often keeps a summary view as well, such as the total quantity and the average cost across all lots. This summary is useful for showing the customer a simple picture. But the summary is not the truth. The lots are the truth, and the summary is worked out from them. When it comes to tax, as we will see, the average is not enough. We need the lots.

Why keep such detail? Because both the price and the date of each lot will matter. The price of each lot decides the gain when that lot is sold. The date of each lot decides how long it was held, which, as we will find in the tax chapter, changes how the gain is treated. A lot, then, is not a minor detail of bookkeeping. It is the true unit of a holding, and the position is built from lots upward.

### When Lots Change on Their Own

We learned in Chapter 4, and again in Chapter 7, that instruments can change through corporate actions, without the customer buying or selling. These events reach down into positions and lots, and a system must adjust the lots when they happen.

A share split is the clearest example. Suppose a company splits its shares two for one, so that every share becomes two. A lot of one hundred shares that cost fifty each becomes a lot of two hundred shares that cost twenty-five each. The quantity has doubled, and the cost per unit has halved, but the total cost of the lot has not changed. The lot has been reshaped, not made larger or smaller in value. The system must apply this to every lot of that share.

A reinvested dividend works differently. When a customer chooses to have their dividends reinvested, the cash from a dividend is used at once to buy a little more of the same instrument. That purchase is a new lot, with its own small quantity, its own price, and its own date. So a holding can grow new lots over time, quietly, from reinvested income, even when the customer never places an order.

Other corporate actions reshape positions in their own ways, such as a merger that turns shares of one company into shares of another. The full handling of these events belongs with our study of market data, where the events themselves are received and understood. Here we need only see the effect: positions and their lots are not fixed once created. They must be kept correct as the world acts upon them.

## When We Sell

So far the customer has only been buying and holding. The interesting questions arrive when they sell. Selling raises two matters we must understand: which lots are being sold, and what the gain from the sale really is.

### Choosing Which Lots to Sell

When a customer sells part of a position, they are selling from lots that were bought at different prices. Which lots? The answer is not automatic, and it matters, because the lots have different costs, and the cost decides the gain.

Return to our customer with two hundred shares, one hundred bought at fifty and one hundred at sixty. Suppose they sell one hundred shares at seventy. If the shares sold are the ones that cost fifty, the gain is twenty a share. If the shares sold are the ones that cost sixty, the gain is only ten a share. Same sale, same price, but a different gain, depending only on which lot we say was sold.

Because the choice changes the gain, there are agreed methods for making it. One method always sells the oldest lots first; it is known by the short name first-in, first-out. Another lets the customer name exactly which lots to sell, which is called specific identification, and which allows a customer to manage their gain on purpose, for example by selling the highest-cost lots to keep the gain small. For funds, a common method is to use the average cost across all lots. The method in use is a real setting on an account, and the system must apply it correctly, because it decides which lots leave and, with them, what gain is produced. We will return to the tax side of this choice in the tax chapter. For now, the point is that a sale is not simply a reduction in quantity. It is the removal of particular lots, chosen by a rule.

### Gains Made Real

The sale also changes the nature of the gain itself, and this is one of the most important ideas in wealth.

While a holding is still held, its gain is not real. We can calculate it: the current value minus the cost basis. But it is only a number. It exists on paper, not as money. And because the value moves with the market, the number moves too. A holding might show a gain of four hundred in the morning and three hundred by the afternoon, without anyone doing anything. This kind of gain, calculated but not yet made real, is called an unrealized gain. It belongs entirely to the uncertain, external side of wealth. It rises and falls with prices the bank does not control, and it could vanish before it is ever collected.

When the holding is sold, everything changes. At the moment of sale, the gain stops being a moving number and becomes a settled fact. The customer sold at a real price, against a real cost, and the gain from that sale is now fixed. It will not move again. This is called a realized gain. In the instant of selling, an uncertain, external number turns into a certain, internal fact, recorded in the bank's own books.

This is the seam of the whole book, seen in a single act. An unrealized gain lives in the world of market prices: external, live, and never certain. A realized gain lives in the world of the ledger: internal, fixed, and beyond doubt. Selling is the act that carries a gain across from one world to the other. It also, in that same instant, creates the tax event we have been pointing toward, because it is the realized gain, not the unrealized one, that the tax authority cares about. A number that only ever existed on paper is not taxed. A gain made real by a sale is.

## From Positions to the Portfolio

A customer rarely holds just one position. They hold many, and the whole set of them, taken together, is called the portfolio. The portfolio is the complete picture of what a customer owns. If the position is the record of one holding, the portfolio is the record of them all.

When a customer looks at their portfolio, they usually see a list of their holdings. For each one, they see the instrument, the quantity held, the current value, and the gain so far. This is the holdings view, and it is one of the plainest and most-used pictures in all of wealth. It is worth noticing that this everyday view is a summary. It shows one line for each position: the whole holding in a company, not the separate lots underneath it. The lot-by-lot detail we studied earlier is still there, and it is still the truth, but it sits below this summary, out of the customer's ordinary sight. The customer sees the shortened, gathered view; the system keeps the full one. Both are needed. The summary answers the everyday question of what is held and how it is doing. The detail answers the exact questions of tax and record that only the lots can settle.

The value of a portfolio follows the same rule we met for the account in Chapter 6. It is not stored. It is worked out, by taking the value of every position, each found from its quantity and its current price, and adding the cash held alongside them. And because every one of those position values moves with the market, the value of the whole portfolio moves constantly, all through the trading day, even when the customer holds everything still.

## Asset Allocation

The holdings view answers what a customer owns, one line at a time. But there is a higher question that a single list cannot answer well: what is the overall shape of the portfolio? For that we turn to asset allocation, one of the most important pictures in all of wealth, and often the very first thing a customer is shown about their money.

Asset allocation is how the value of a portfolio is divided across kinds of holding. Rather than listing every position, it gathers them into groups and shows the share of the whole that each group makes. The most common grouping is by asset class, the families we met in Chapter 7: how much of the portfolio's value is in equities, how much in fixed income, how much in cash, how much in alternatives. Seen this way, a long list of holdings becomes a short, clear summary of how a customer is invested.

Asset class is only one way to group. The same portfolio can be divided along other lines, and each tells us something different. It can be split by the part of the economy each holding belongs to, so that we see how much is in one industry and how much in another. It can be split by region, so that we see how much is invested at home and how much abroad. It can be split by currency, or shown holding by holding. Each of these is the same act: take the positions, gather them by some attribute, and add up the value in each group as a share of the whole. Allocation, in every case, is a grouped view of the portfolio, worked out from the positions and their current values.

Allocation matters because it describes the shape of a customer's risk far better than any single holding does. A portfolio spread evenly across many kinds of asset carries a different risk from one placed entirely in a single industry. This is why allocation is usually set as a goal in advance. A customer, often with an adviser, decides on a target allocation: a chosen shape they wish the portfolio to hold, such as a certain share in equities and a certain share in fixed income. This target is frequently drawn from a model portfolio, the standard mix we mentioned in Chapter 4. The portfolio's actual allocation is then compared against this target, and the gap between the two is what guides decisions about what to buy and sell.

Here we meet again the quiet feature we noted for the portfolio's value. Because allocation is measured by value, and values move, the actual allocation drifts on its own. Suppose a portfolio has a target of half its value in equities. If equities rise while the other holdings do not, the equity share slowly grows past a half, without a single trade being made. The actual allocation has drifted away from its target, carried only by the market. Bringing the actual allocation back toward its target is a task called rebalancing, which we will study when we reach advice. For now, we need only hold two ideas. First, allocation is the portfolio's overall shape, computed by grouping positions by value. Second, that shape has both a target, which is chosen and stays put, and an actual, which is measured and drifts. The distance between the chosen shape and the real one is among the most watched numbers in a customer's wealth.

## The True Record

We can now see why the position, humble as it looks, is the true record of a customer's wealth. It holds far more than a quantity. Underneath, it holds its lots, each remembering what was paid and when. From those facts come the answers to the questions that matter most: what a holding is worth now, how much it has gained, what would be owed if it were sold, and how it should be counted for tax. The account told us where wealth is kept. The instruments told us what is owned. The position, with its lots and its cost, tells us the full story of a holding, and the portfolio, with its allocation, gathers those stories into a single shape.

The position also shows us the two truths of this book held together in a single place. The quantity and the cost are internal and certain: the bank knows exactly how many units are held and exactly what was paid for them. These are settled facts of the past. The current value and the unrealized gain are external and uncertain: they depend on prices set outside the bank, which move without pause and can never be known for sure. The position sits across this divide. Half of it is fixed history; half of it is restless present.

And that restless present has a source we have named many times but not yet examined on its own. Every uncertain value, every moving gain, every drifting allocation traces back to one thing: the price of an instrument, set by the market, arriving from outside. We have relied on that price throughout these chapters, and taken it as given. It is time to stop taking it as given. In the next chapter we study market data directly: where prices come from, what they really are, and why the simple idea of the price of a thing turns out to be one of the hardest ideas in all of wealth.
