# Chapter 9: Market Data

Through the last three chapters, we leaned on a single word without ever examining it: price. The account's value came from prices. A position's worth, and its gain, came from prices. The portfolio's value and its allocation came from prices. Each time, we took the price of an instrument as a thing that simply existed, a number we could reach for when we needed it. Now we stop and look at that number directly. It is time to ask where prices come from, what they really are, and why they cause so much difficulty.

This is the chapter the whole book has been moving toward. In Chapter 1 we said that wealth is the hardest of the three categories of banking, because its truth is external, live, and uncertain. Those three words have been present in every chapter since. Here they finally come into full view. Market data is the name for the information about what instruments are worth and at what price they trade. It is where the external, live, uncertain truth of wealth actually lives. And it is, without exaggeration, one of the hardest parts of building wealth software.

We will find that the simple phrase the price of a thing hides more than it says. We will see that an instrument does not have one price. We will see where prices come from, and that some of them are not really measured at all, but estimated. We will see that a price is never still, and that any price we hold is already a little out of date. And we will see how instruments themselves change through corporate actions, reshaping not only today but the whole past. By the end, the three words from Chapter 1 will no longer be abstract. They will be concrete problems we can name.

## What "the Price" Really Is

We begin with the number itself. When we say an instrument has a price, we picture one value: the price of a share, of a bond, of a fund. This picture is wrong, or at least far too simple. At any single moment, an instrument does not have one price. It has several numbers at once, and they mean different things.

The most familiar is the last trade price. This is the price at which the most recent trade in the instrument actually took place. It is a real event: two parties agreed, and the trade happened at that price. But notice that it is already in the past. It tells us what someone paid a moment ago, not what we could pay or receive now.

For that, we look at two other numbers. The bid is the highest price that a buyer is, right now, willing to pay. The ask, sometimes called the offer, is the lowest price that a seller is, right now, willing to accept. These two describe what is possible at this instant. If we wanted to sell, we could expect something close to the bid. If we wanted to buy, we would expect to pay something close to the ask.

The bid and the ask are almost never equal. The ask is higher than the bid, and the gap between them is called the spread. The spread is small for instruments that are heavily traded, where many buyers and sellers crowd close together. It is wide for instruments that trade rarely, where buyers and sellers are far apart. The spread is a real cost of trading, and it is also a sign of how easy an instrument is to trade at all. Alongside the bid and ask, there is usually a size: how much can be bought or sold at each of those prices. And beneath the best bid and ask lies a deeper list of other buyers and sellers at worse prices, called the order book, of which the bid and ask are only the top.

We can gather the current state of an instrument's prices into a single picture, called a quote.

```
Quote
  instrument     which instrument this quote is for
  last           the price of the most recent trade
  bid            the highest price a buyer will now pay
  ask            the lowest price a seller will now accept
  bidSize        how much can be sold at the bid
  askSize        how much can be bought at the ask
  asOf           the instant this quote was true
```

As before, this describes what the domain holds, not code to run. This picture already tells us something important. There is no single true price. There is a bundle of numbers, each answering a different question. This means that whenever we need a price, we must choose which number to use, and the right choice depends on what we are doing. To show a customer what their holding is worth, a system might use the last trade price, or the midpoint between the bid and the ask, or the bid if it wishes to be careful. To estimate what a sale would bring, the bid is the honest number. To estimate what a purchase would cost, the ask is. The same holding can be worth slightly different amounts depending on which of these we pick, and a wealth system must know, in every place, which price it means and why. Here, at once, is the uncertainty from Chapter 1, made plain: not confusion, but the simple fact that the price is not one number, and we must always say which we mean.

## Where Prices Come From

If the bank does not set these prices, where do they come from? The answer is: from outside, and from several places, and not always in the same way.

For instruments that trade on an exchange, such as shares and exchange-traded funds, the prices come from the exchange itself, or from the many trading venues where the instrument changes hands. Every trade and every change in the best bid or ask is reported, and this vast flow of reports is the raw material of market data. Because there are many venues, and because consuming their reports directly is complex and costly, most wealth firms do not connect to each exchange themselves. They buy their market data from specialised providers, whose business is to collect it from all the venues, clean it, put it into a common form, and pass it on. So the price a wealth system shows has usually passed through several steps before it arrives.

This brings us to a fact that is easy to overlook and important to grasp. Market data is not free. It is a product, sold under licence, and it can be expensive. Exchanges charge for their data, and there are rules about who may see prices as they happen and who may only see them delayed. Showing a customer a live, up-to-the-second price carries a cost and a set of permissions behind it. This is a real constraint on any wealth system, and it shapes what prices it can show, to whom, and how fresh they may be.

Not every instrument is priced in the same clean way, and this matters a great deal. For shares and exchange-traded funds, there is a constant stream of real trades, so the price is measured directly. For a mutual fund, as we saw in Chapter 7, there is no live price at all; the fund itself works out a single value once a day, after the market closes. For a bond, the situation is harder still. Most bonds trade rarely, so there is often no recent trade to point to. Instead, a price is estimated by a pricing service, which looks at similar bonds and at market conditions and produces a considered value. This is not a measured price but a judged one, and it is called an evaluated price. For alternatives, the value may be nothing more than an occasional estimate, made by appraisal.

Here we see plainly the spectrum we described in Chapter 7. The external truth does not arrive in one form. For some instruments it is measured, trade by trade, second by second. For others it is calculated once a day. For others still it is only estimated, now and then, by judgment. When we say that value comes from outside the bank, we must remember that outside is not a single, reliable voice. It speaks clearly for some instruments and only faintly, or as a guess, for others.

## Prices Never Stop

We have seen that a price is a bundle of numbers, and that it comes from outside. Now we meet the property that causes the most trouble of all. A price does not hold still. It moves, constantly, for as long as the market is open.

For a heavily traded instrument, the numbers can change many times every second. A new trade happens, and the last price changes. A buyer or seller changes their mind, and the bid or the ask shifts. Across a whole market, this produces an enormous, ceaseless flow of updates. Market data of this kind is not a value we ask for once and keep. It arrives as a stream: a continuous flow of new prices, one after another, without pause, throughout the trading day.

This changes how we must think about a price entirely. In retail, a balance was a value the bank stored, and it changed only when the bank recorded a movement. A price is nothing like this. There is no single stored price to keep correct. There is only a stream of updates, and a value we happen to hold at some instant, which the stream is already making out of date.

This leads to an idea an engineer must keep clearly in mind: staleness. The moment we take a price and hold it, whether to show it, to value a holding, or to make a decision, it begins to age. A price is true only for the instant it describes. A second later, it may already be wrong, because the market has moved on. Every price we work with carries a hidden question: how old is it, and is it fresh enough for what we are using it for? A live trading screen needs prices that are only a fraction of a second old. A monthly statement can happily use a price from the close of a chosen day. The same instrument, the same kind of number, but a completely different demand for freshness.

This is why market data is one of the hardest problems in wealth software. We are asked to show and use a value whose true source never stops changing. We must decide, for each use, how fresh the value must be. We must keep our held values current against the endless stream, updating them as new prices arrive, and knowing which of our many uses each update should reach. And we must always be honest about the fact that any value we display is a snapshot, correct for the instant it was taken and drifting out of date from that instant onward. How exactly a system meets this challenge is a matter of design, and belongs to another book. But the shape of the challenge belongs here, because it is a fact of the domain. To work with prices is to work with something that will not sit still.

## Live, Delayed, and Closing Prices

The endless stream of prices during trading hours is not the only way market data reaches us. There are, in fact, two quite different worlds of price, and a wealth system lives in both.

The first is the live, moving world we have just described. Within it, there are choices of freshness that follow directly from the cost and permissions we mentioned. Real-time data shows prices as they happen, to the instant. It is the most useful and the most expensive, and its use is controlled. Delayed data shows prices as they were a set time ago, often around a quarter of an hour behind. It is far cheaper, and it is what many ordinary customers are shown, because a price that is a little old is good enough for simply watching a holding. The difference between real-time and delayed is not a difference in the prices themselves, only in how fresh they are allowed to be.

The second world is the official, settled world of the closing price. At the end of each trading day, when the market closes, a single official price is fixed for each instrument for that day. This closing price does not move. It is the agreed, recorded value of the instrument for the day, and it plays a role the live price cannot. Much of the official record of wealth is built on closing prices, not live ones. A statement values a portfolio at the closing prices of a chosen date. Performance is measured from close to close. A mutual fund's daily value is a closing value. Tax is worked out on settled, dated prices. So while the live stream governs what a customer sees moment to moment, the closing price governs the formal record: what a holding is officially worth on a given day.

We should add that the market is not always open. Instruments trade during set hours, and outside those hours there is usually no continuous trading and so no live quote. Yet value does not wait for the market to open. News breaks overnight. Events happen. Other markets, in other parts of the world, move. When the market opens again, the first price may sit some distance from the previous close, because the world changed while trading was shut. This is another face of the same truth from Chapter 1: value is live, and it can move even when no price is being quoted at all.

## Corporate Actions

There is one more kind of market data, different from prices, that we have promised twice to explain, and now must. Instruments do not only change in price. They sometimes change in their very nature, through events called corporate actions. We met these briefly in Chapter 4, and we saw their effect on holdings in Chapter 8. Here we look at the events themselves.

A corporate action is something a company or an issuer does that changes an instrument or the holding of it. We have already met the common ones. In a share split, each share becomes a set number of new shares, and the price per share changes to match. In a dividend, a company pays a share of its profits to those who hold it, either as cash or as more shares. In a merger, one company joins another, and the shares of one are turned into shares of the other, or into cash. There are many more, such as the change of a company's name or trading symbol, or the splitting off of part of a company into a new one. Some corporate actions even require the holder to make a choice, such as whether to accept an offer, and the system must gather that choice and act on it.

These events arrive as their own kind of data, and a wealth system must receive them, understand them, and apply their effects correctly and on time. Applying them touches many of the things we have studied: quantities change, prices change, holdings and their lots must be adjusted, and cash may move.

One effect of corporate actions is easy to miss and important to get right. They reshape not only the present but the past. Consider a share that splits two for one. Before the split, it traded at one hundred. After the split, the same value is held as twice as many shares at fifty each. If we later look back at the price history and see one hundred yesterday and fifty today, it appears that the instrument lost half its value overnight, which is false. Nothing was lost; the shares were simply divided. To keep the history honest, the past prices must be adjusted for the split, so that they can be fairly compared with the present. This adjusting of history is a quiet but essential duty of market data, and it matters wherever the past is used, such as in a price chart or in the measurement of performance over time.

## The Price Behind Everything

We can now look back and see how far the simple word price has taken us. When we began this chapter, price was a single number we reached for without thought. It is nothing of the kind.

We have found that an instrument has no one price, but a bundle of numbers, each meaning something different, from which we must always choose. That is the uncertainty of Chapter 1, made real: not confusion, but the plain fact that we must say which price we mean. We have found that prices come from outside the bank, through exchanges and providers, at a cost and under permission, and that for many instruments the price is not measured but judged. That is the external truth of Chapter 1, made real: the value comes from beyond us, in many forms, some of them only estimates. And we have found that prices never stop moving, that any value we hold is a snapshot already ageing, and that keeping our world current against an endless stream is among the hardest tasks in wealth software. That is the live truth of Chapter 1, made real: the price will not sit still.

External, live, uncertain. Three words that, at the start of this book, were a claim we asked the reader to accept. They are now three concrete realities, each with a face we have seen. Almost everything that makes wealth harder than retail or lending flows from this one source. The value of a holding, the gain on it, the worth of a portfolio, the shape of its allocation: all of them rest on prices that come from outside, that move without pause, and that are never quite one certain number.

There is a natural next question. We have talked about buying and selling many times, and about the prices at which trades happen. But we have not yet followed a trade itself. How does a customer's wish to buy or sell become a real change in what they own, out in this moving market we have now seen? That is the subject of the next chapter, where we study the life of a trade, from the order a customer places to the settled change in their holdings. Having understood the market that prices live in, we are ready to step into it.
