# Chapter 10: The Life of a Trade

In the last chapter we came to know the market: the moving, uncertain place where prices live. We watched prices flow and change, and we saw that no single number ever holds still. Now we follow something into that market. A customer decides to buy, or to sell. This chapter is about what happens next, and it turns out to be a longer journey than we might expect.

In retail, buying meant a payment: money moved from one place to another, and that was the whole of it. A trade in wealth is not like this. When a customer buys an instrument, two things must change places at once. Money must go from the buyer to the seller, and ownership of the instrument must go from the seller to the buyer. There is another party on the far side, whose wish is the opposite of ours. There is a moving price to meet. And there is a wait, of days, before the deal is truly done. A trade is where a customer's wish becomes a real change in what they own, and it passes, on the way, through the very uncertainty we studied in the last chapter.

We will follow a trade through its stages. It begins as an order, an instruction to buy or sell. It is sent to the market, where it is executed against another party. It is then cleared, confirmed and made ready. It is finally settled, when money and ownership at last change places. And underneath all of this sits custody, the safekeeping of what a customer owns. Each stage has its own state and its own difficulties, and together they show why a trade is so much more than a payment.

## The Order

Everything begins with an order. An order is an instruction to buy or to sell: this instrument, this many units, in this way. It is the customer's wish, written down precisely enough for a system to act on it. An order records, at the least, which instrument is wanted, whether it is a buy or a sell, and how much. But there is one more choice that shapes everything about how the order behaves, and it deserves our attention: the kind of order.

The two most important kinds sit in a revealing contrast, and the contrast is the same certain-against-uncertain tension we have met throughout this book.

A market order says: buy or sell at once, at whatever the price now is. It asks for speed and certainty of execution. The customer will get their trade, and quickly. But they do not control the price. In the moving market of the last chapter, the price when the order arrives may differ from the price the customer saw a moment before. A market order gives certain execution and an uncertain price.

A limit order says the opposite: buy or sell only at this price, or better. The customer names a price and will not accept worse. This gives them control of the price. But now the execution is uncertain. If the market never reaches the named price, the order simply does not happen. A limit order gives a certain price and uncertain execution.

This is the first real decision in any trade, and it is a choice about which uncertainty to accept. Do we want our trade for sure, and accept whatever price the market gives? Or do we want our price for sure, and accept that the trade may not happen? There are other kinds of order built from these ideas, such as a stop order, which waits, doing nothing, until the price crosses a chosen level, and only then becomes a market order, often used to limit a loss. But the two that matter most are the market order and the limit order, and the trade-off between them.

An order also carries a sense of how long it should live. Some orders last only for the day, and are cancelled if not filled by the close. Others stand until they are filled or the customer cancels them. This is called the order's time in force, and it decides how long the customer's wish waits in the market before it lapses.

We can gather these into the shape of an order.

```
Order
  instrument     which instrument to trade
  side           buy or sell
  quantity       how many units
  orderType      market, limit, and so on
  limitPrice     the price limit, for a limit order
  timeInForce    how long the order stays alive
  status         its current stage, from placed to filled
```

As before, this describes what the domain holds, not code to run. Notice the last field: status. An order is not a single event that happens and is over. It is a thing with a life, which moves through stages, and the status records where in that life it currently sits. Following those stages is the rest of this chapter.

## Execution

Once an order is placed, it must reach a place where it can be matched with someone who wants the opposite. The customer's order goes first to their broker, who sends it on to a market venue where buyers and sellers meet. The choice of where to send it is not trivial; a firm has a duty to seek the best reasonably available terms for the customer, a duty often called best execution, which we will meet again among the rules. Once the order reaches the market, it waits, if it is a limit order, or is carried out at once, if it is a market order.

The carrying out is called execution. To execute an order is to match it with a party on the other side and agree a trade at a price. The moment of execution is the moment the customer's wish becomes a fact. Before it, there was only an instruction and a hope. After it, there is a real trade, at a real price, that has truly happened.

Execution is rarely as simple as one clean match. A large order may not be filled all at once. There may be a party willing to sell three hundred at one price, and another willing to sell the rest at a slightly different price. So a single order can be filled in several pieces, at several prices, one after another. Each piece is called a fill, and an order may have many. Until the whole quantity is filled, the order is only partly done, and a system must track how much has been filled, at what prices, and how much still remains. The customer's true price for the order is then the average across all its fills.

Here the moving market of the last chapter shows itself directly. Because prices shift constantly, the price at which an order executes is often not quite the price the customer expected when they placed it. This difference, between the expected price and the price actually achieved, is called slippage, and it is a normal part of trading in a live market. A large order can even move the price against itself as it fills, buying up the cheapest offers and reaching for more expensive ones. This is why the uncertainty of price we studied is not an abstract worry. It appears, in plain money, every time a market order is executed.

## After the Trade: Clearing and Settlement

When an order is executed, it is tempting to think the trade is over. It is not. The two sides have agreed, but nothing final has yet changed places. Money has not moved. Ownership has not moved. Between the agreement and the true completion lie two stages that most customers never see, but that a wealth system must understand: clearing and settlement.

### Clearing

Clearing is the work that happens after a trade is agreed and before it is completed. Its purpose is to make the trade safe and ready. Several things happen in it.

First, the details of the trade are confirmed. Both sides recorded the trade, and their records must be checked against each other to be sure they agree: the same instrument, the same quantity, the same price. A mismatch must be found and resolved before the trade can go on.

Second, and most importantly, the risk that one side fails to keep its part is managed. In many markets, a special institution steps into the middle of every trade for exactly this purpose. It becomes the buyer to every seller and the seller to every buyer. This institution is called a central counterparty, or a clearing house, and its effect is powerful. Once it stands in the middle, the customer no longer depends on the party they actually traded with. If that party fails, the clearing house still ensures the customer receives what they are owed. This is why a customer never has to worry about who was on the other side of their trade. An institution has guaranteed it.

Clearing also gathers many trades together and reduces them. If a party bought and sold the same instrument many times in a day, there is no need to move every share back and forth. Only the net result needs to move. This gathering and reducing is called netting, and it makes the final step far smaller than the day's trading would suggest.

### Settlement

Settlement is the moment the trade is truly finished. Here, at last, money passes from the buyer to the seller, and ownership of the instrument passes from the seller to the buyer. Only now is the deal complete. Everything before this was agreement and preparation. Settlement is the act itself.

The most surprising thing about settlement, to someone new to it, is that it does not happen at once. It happens a small, set number of business days after the trade is made. The day the trade is agreed is called the trade date. The day it settles is called the settlement date, and it comes a little later. This gap has grown shorter over the years, and in some markets it is now only a single day, but it has not vanished. Between the two dates, the trade sits in an unfinished state: agreed, guaranteed, but not yet complete. The buyer has committed to buy but does not yet fully own. The seller has committed to sell but has not yet fully given it up.

This gap has real consequences for a system. There are, in effect, two moments when a trade might be reflected in a customer's records: when it is agreed, and when it settles. A system must decide which it shows, and when, and must be able to represent a holding that is bought but not yet settled. It must also handle the unhappy case where settlement does not go as planned, where a seller cannot deliver the instrument, or a buyer cannot pay. Such a case is called a settlement failure, and while it is not common, a wealth system cannot pretend it never happens. The neat picture of money and ownership changing places on the settlement date is the usual case, not the only one.

## Custody

Beneath every holding, and every trade, lies a question we have so far taken for granted: where are a customer's instruments actually kept? The answer is custody, the safekeeping of what a customer owns.

In earlier times, owning a share might have meant holding a paper certificate. This is almost never so now. A customer's instruments are held for them, in electronic records, by institutions built for the purpose. At the base of it all sits a kind of central register where the true electronic record of who owns what is kept. Above that sit the custodians and brokers who hold instruments on behalf of their customers.

This layering has an effect worth understanding. Very often, a customer's instruments are not registered directly in the customer's own name. They are held in the name of the broker or a nominee, on the customer's behalf. The customer is what is called the beneficial owner: the one who truly owns the instrument and enjoys its value, its dividends, and its rights, even though their name is not the one on the central record. This arrangement makes trading and settlement far smoother, because holdings can move between accounts without rewriting a central register each time.

Custody is where the certain side of wealth, from Chapter 1, finally rests. Recall that we said the quantity a customer holds is internal and known for sure, while its value is external and uncertain. It is in the custody records that this certain quantity actually lives. The number of shares a customer owns is a fact held safe in these records, exact and beyond doubt. Custody is also where the corporate actions of the last chapter take effect: it is through the custody chain that a dividend reaches the right owner, and a split changes the right holdings. The safekeeping of instruments is quiet work, rarely seen by the customer, but it is the ground on which everything a customer owns finally stands.

## From Wish to Holding

We have followed a trade from beginning to end. It started as a wish, written into an order. It passed, as a market or a limit order, into the moving market, and was executed there, often in pieces and at a price a little different from the one expected. It was cleared, its details confirmed and its completion guaranteed by an institution that stood in the middle. It was settled, days later, when money and ownership at last changed places. And all the while, the instruments themselves rested in the safekeeping of custody.

Look back at how much this asked, compared with a retail payment. A payment moved money in one direction and was done. A trade moved money one way and ownership the other, met a party with the opposite wish, faced a price that would not hold still, waited days to complete, and relied on institutions to confirm it, guarantee it, and hold what was owned. This is the pattern we have seen again and again. Wealth stands on the foundation of retail, using the same movement of money to pay for what is bought, but it adds so much on top that the result is a different thing entirely.

We can also see, once more, the deep theme of this book at work. A trade is another crossing from the uncertain to the certain. An order is a wish held in the uncertain world of moving prices. At the moment of execution, that wish becomes a fixed fact: a real trade, at a real price, that has happened and will not change. And at settlement, that fact becomes final and complete. The customer's intention, begun in uncertainty, ends as a settled holding, recorded and safe.

With trades understood, a new question comes into view. As a customer buys and sells, and as prices move beneath their holdings, how are they, and we, to know whether they are doing well? Money has come in and gone out. Trades have been made. Prices have risen and fallen. Somewhere in all this movement is the answer to a simple question that turns out to be surprisingly hard: how well has this portfolio performed? That is the question we take up in the next chapter.
