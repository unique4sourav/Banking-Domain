# Chapter 11: Performance

We come to a question that sounds simple and is not. A customer looks at their portfolio and asks: how am I doing? Am I ahead, or behind? Is the money growing well, or badly? This question, so natural to ask, has troubled us since Chapter 4, where we first warned that it was harder than it looks. Now we face it fully.

The measure of how well a portfolio has done is called its performance, and the basic idea within it is the return: how much the portfolio has grown or shrunk over a period, given as a percentage. Retail and lending never needed such a thing. A savings balance simply is what it is. A loan follows its schedule. But an investment is meant to grow, and the whole point of it is to grow well rather than badly, so we must be able to measure the growing. That is what this chapter is about.

We will find something we have found before, in a new form. Just as an instrument had no single price, a portfolio has no single return. The question how did we do turns out to hold two different questions inside it, which need two different answers. We will meet both, and see why they can disagree, sometimes sharply. We will then see that a return means little on its own, and needs two more things to make sense of it: a period it is measured over, and something to be compared against. Finally, we will see how a return can be broken open to show where it came from. Performance rests on everything we have built so far, the positions, the prices, the trades, and it is where all of it is finally judged.

## What Return Is, and Why It Is Hard

Let us begin with the simplest idea of return, and watch it break.

If a portfolio was worth one hundred at the start of a year and one hundred and ten at the end, we would say it returned ten percent. We take the gain, ten, and express it as a share of what we started with, one hundred. This is the plain return, and when nothing else happens, it is exactly right.

But something else almost always happens. Customers add money to their portfolios, and take money out. And the moment they do, the simple return becomes wrong, in a way that is easy to miss.

Suppose a portfolio is worth one hundred at the start of the year and one hundred and fifty at the end. It is tempting to say it returned fifty percent. But suppose that, during the year, the customer added forty of their own money. Then the growth was not fifty. The value rose by fifty, yes, but forty of that rise was simply the customer's own money being placed into the account. The investments themselves produced only ten of the rise, not fifty. To say the portfolio returned fifty percent would be to praise the investments for money the customer put in with their own hand.

Here is the whole difficulty of performance, in one picture. A portfolio's value changes for two quite different reasons. It changes because the investments rise and fall, which is the market at work. And it changes because the customer adds and removes money, which is the customer at work. When we measure performance, we usually want to know about the first and not the second. We want to know how the investments did, not how much money the customer happened to pay in. So we must find a way to separate the two, to pull apart the market's effect from the customer's own deposits and withdrawals. Every serious measure of return is, at heart, a way of doing this separation. And as we will now see, there is more than one way to do it, because there is more than one thing we might want to know.

## Two Questions, Two Returns

When we ask how a portfolio did, we might be asking either of two questions, and they are not the same.

The first question is about the investments. How well were they chosen and managed? Did the money, wherever it was invested, grow well? This question should not depend on when the customer happened to add or remove money, because that timing is usually the customer's doing, not the manager's.

The second question is about the customer. What return did their actual money earn, given everything, including their own decisions about when to put money in and take it out? This question should depend on that timing, because the timing was part of what the customer did, and it affected the real result in their pocket.

These are different questions, and each has its own measure.

### The Return of the Investments

The first question, about the investments alone, is answered by the time-weighted return. Its purpose is to measure how the investments performed, with the effect of the customer's deposits and withdrawals removed entirely.

The idea behind it is simple to describe. We cut the whole period into pieces, making a cut at every point where money was added or removed. Within each piece, no money went in or out, so within each piece the plain return is honest. We work out the return of each piece on its own, and then we join the pieces together, one after another, to get the return for the whole period. Because each piece is measured on whatever value happened to be there, and the deposits and withdrawals only ever land at the cuts between pieces, the size of those deposits and withdrawals never distorts the result. What remains is a clean measure of how the investments themselves did, as if they had been left to grow on a fixed sum.

This is why the time-weighted return is the standard measure for judging and comparing investments. A fund, a manager, a strategy: all are measured this way, because it is fair. Two managers can be compared even if their customers added and removed money in completely different patterns, because the measure has taken all of that out. It answers, cleanly, the question: how good was the investing?

### The Return of the Customer's Money

The second question, about the customer's own result, is answered by the money-weighted return, which is also known as the internal rate of return. Its purpose is the opposite of the first. Rather than removing the effect of the customer's deposits and withdrawals, it takes full account of them, because they are part of what the customer actually experienced.

The idea here is to find the single rate of return that, applied to the customer's money over the exact time each part of it was invested, would produce the final result. Money that was in the portfolio for longer, or in larger amount, counts for more. Money added late counts for less, because it had less time to work. In this way, the measure weights the return by how much money was present, and for how long, which is exactly what the name money-weighted means. The result is the return the customer's actual money earned, timing and all.

This measure answers a different and more personal question: not how good was the investing, but how did I, in the end, actually do? It reflects the customer's own choices about when to commit money. If they added a large sum just before a good period, their money-weighted return is lifted by it. If they added just before a bad one, it is dragged down. This is not a flaw. It is the honest truth of their experience, which the first measure, by design, leaves out.

### When They Disagree

Because these two measures answer different questions, they can give different numbers for the very same portfolio over the very same period. Sometimes the difference is small. Sometimes it is large, and it is worth seeing how.

Consider a customer whose portfolio moves through a year like this.

| Event | Portfolio value |
|---|---|
| Start of year | 100 |
| Investments rise 20 percent | 120 |
| Customer adds 100 | 220 |
| Investments fall 10 percent | 198 |

Let us ask both questions of this year.

First, how did the investments do? They rose twenty percent, then fell ten percent. Joining these two pieces gives the time-weighted return: a rise of twenty percent followed by a fall of ten percent works out to a gain of eight percent over the year. By this measure, the investments did well. The money, wherever it sat, grew by eight percent.

Now, how did the customer actually do? They put in one hundred at the start, and another hundred in the middle, so they committed two hundred of their own money in all. At the end, the portfolio is worth one hundred and ninety-eight. They put in two hundred and have one hundred and ninety-eight: a small loss. Their money-weighted return is slightly below zero. And the reason is plain in the table. The customer had only one hundred invested during the good period, when investments rose, but two hundred and twenty invested during the bad period, when they fell. More of their money was present for the fall than for the rise. The investments gained eight percent, but the customer, because of when they added money, lost a little.

Both numbers are correct. The eight percent is the true return of the investments. The small loss is the true return of the customer's money. Neither is wrong, and neither is the return. They are the answers to two different questions, and a wealth system that reports performance must know which question it is answering, and say so plainly, or it will mislead without meaning to.

## Over What Period, and Compared to What

Even once we have chosen which return to measure, a return on its own still does not tell us much. It needs two more things to become meaningful: a period, and a comparison.

### Always Over a Period

A return is always the return over some stretch of time. The same portfolio has one return over a month, another over a year, another over five years. A return without a period attached is not an answer at all, only half of one. So the first thing to fix is always: over what time?

This raises a small difficulty when we wish to compare returns over stretches of different length. A gain of two percent over three months is not the same as a gain of two percent over three years, though the number is the same. To compare them fairly, we often express returns on a common basis, usually a single year, a practice called annualizing. A short period's return is scaled up to what it would amount to over a year; a long period's is scaled down to a yearly average. We should also keep clear the difference between the total return over a whole span, and the average yearly return within it. A portfolio might have grown by sixty percent over five years in total, which is about ten percent for each year on average. Both are true, and they are different numbers, and we must always know which one is being shown.

### Always Against a Benchmark

A return also needs something to be compared against, because a number alone cannot tell us whether it is good. Suppose a portfolio returned eight percent in a year. Is that good? We cannot say, until we know what else was possible. If the market as a whole rose fifteen percent that year, then eight percent was poor: the portfolio fell well behind what it might easily have earned. If the market fell five percent, then eight percent was excellent.

This is why performance is measured against a benchmark. A benchmark is a standard to compare against, usually a market index: a number that represents how a broad set of investments, or a particular slice of the market, performed over the same period. By setting the portfolio's return beside the benchmark's, we can see whether the portfolio did better or worse than the market it lives in. The portfolio's return minus the benchmark's return is often the number that truly matters, and it shows whether the investing added anything beyond what the market itself provided.

The benchmark must be chosen with care, so that it is a fair match for the portfolio. Comparing a portfolio of bonds against an index of shares would tell us nothing useful, because the two are unlike. A fair benchmark resembles the portfolio in the kinds of thing it holds. And there is one more caution to carry, which we will only name. A higher return is not automatically a better one, if it was won by taking far greater risk. Return should always be seen alongside the risk that was taken to earn it, and there are measures that weigh the two together. Their detail is beyond us here, but the principle should stay with us: performance is return and risk together, never return alone.

## Explaining the Return: Attribution

We have a return. We know its period, and how it compares to a benchmark. One question remains, and it is the deepest: why? Why did the portfolio earn what it earned? What produced the result, and what would we change to do better? The work of answering this is called attribution.

Attribution breaks a return down into the causes that produced it. Rather than a single number for the whole portfolio, it assigns portions of the return to the decisions behind it. Consider a portfolio that did better than its benchmark. Attribution might show that part of the advantage came from choosing to hold more in one area of the market than the benchmark did, an area that happened to do well. Another part might have come from picking particular holdings within an area that did better than their neighbours. Yet another part might have come from other effects entirely. Added together, these portions explain the whole of the difference between the portfolio and its benchmark.

Attribution turns a bare result into understanding. It is the difference between knowing that a portfolio did better than the market and knowing why it did, which decisions helped and which hurt. The methods for doing this carefully can be involved, and their full detail belongs to a more specialised study. For our purposes, it is enough to hold the idea: a return is not only a number to be reported, but a result that can be taken apart and explained, so that the reasons behind it can be seen and learned from.

## How Did We Do?

We began this chapter with a question that seemed simple: how did we do? We can now see how much lay hidden inside it.

There was, first, no single answer, because there was no single question. To ask how a portfolio did is to ask either how the investments performed or how the customer's own money fared, and these can differ, sometimes greatly. We saw a case where the investments gained eight percent while the customer, by the timing of their deposits, lost a little. Both were true. This is a shape we have met again and again in wealth. An instrument had no single price, but a bundle of numbers from which we had to choose. A portfolio has no single return, but two, and we must always say which we mean.

Yet there is a difference worth noticing between this uncertainty and the ones before it. When prices moved and refused to settle, the difficulty lay in the world itself: the value was genuinely unsettled, out there in the market. Here the difficulty is of another kind. The facts are known. We have the values, from the prices we studied. We have the flows, from the trades we followed. Nothing is uncertain in the world. The difficulty is in the meaning: in deciding what question we are answering, and choosing the measure that answers it. Wealth is hard not only because its facts move, but because even its settled facts can be read in more than one honest way.

Performance also showed us how everything we have built stands together. To measure a return, we needed the positions, to know what was held. We needed the prices, to know what it was worth. We needed the trades and the flows of money, to know what the customer did. Performance is the height from which all of it is finally surveyed, the place where positions, prices, and trades are gathered into a single judgment of how well a portfolio has done.

But a judgment of how well we have done is not the end of the matter, because we do not keep all of what we earn. A share of every gain, once it is made real, is owed elsewhere. The customer's true reward is not the return we have measured, but what is left of it after the tax authority has taken its part. And so, having measured what was gained, we turn next to what must be given. The subject of the next chapter is tax.
