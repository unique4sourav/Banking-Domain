# Chapter 12: Tax

At the close of the last chapter we made a hard admission: the return we work so carefully to measure is not, in the end, what the customer keeps. Between the gain and the customer stands the tax authority, whose share must be paid before the reward is truly theirs. This chapter is about that share. It is about how wealth is taxed, and about the long trail of consequences that tax leaves through everything a customer does.

Wealth is a taxable activity in a way that retail and lending are not. A savings account earns a little interest, and the tax on it is simple. A loan, for the borrower, is barely a tax matter at all. But in wealth, nearly every action has a tax consequence. Selling can create a gain to be taxed, or a loss that saves tax. Holding can produce income that is taxed as it arrives. The kind of account a holding sits in changes whether, and when, any of this applies. A wealth system cannot treat tax as an afterthought. It must track the tax meaning of events as they happen, and report them faithfully, because the customer's true reward, and the law itself, depend on getting it right.

Before we begin, one honest caution. The exact rules of tax differ from country to country, and they change from year to year. We will not learn any particular country's tax law here, and nothing in this chapter is tax advice. What we will learn is the shape of the thing: the kinds of tax event, the ideas that govern them, and the demands they place on a wealth system. That shape holds broadly, even as the details differ.

Tax will also draw together threads we have left unfinished. In Chapter 6 we said the tax treatment of an account belonged to this chapter. In Chapter 8 we said the reason lots carry a date, and the reason it matters which lot we sell, belonged here too. Now we gather these in. We will find that tax comes from two sources, gains and income; that it cares only about what is real and settled, never about what is merely on paper; that it can be managed, within limits; that it depends entirely on the kind of account; and that all of it must be reported.

## Gains and Losses

The first and largest source of tax in wealth comes from buying and selling. When a customer sells an investment, they may sell it for more than it cost them, or for less. The difference is a capital gain if it is positive, or a capital loss if it is negative. A gain is generally taxable. A loss, as we will see, is generally useful. Understanding this one source well is most of understanding tax in wealth.

### Only Realized Gains Are Taxed

We must begin with a distinction we already know, because it is the foundation of everything here. In Chapter 8 we separated the unrealized gain, which exists only on paper while a holding is still held, from the realized gain, which becomes a settled fact at the moment of sale. Tax rests entirely on this distinction. Only realized gains are taxed. Unrealized gains are not.

This is worth pausing on, because its effect is large. A customer may hold an investment that has risen enormously, worth many times what they paid. On paper, they have a great gain. Yet they owe no tax on it, none at all, for as long as they simply hold it. The tax appears only when they sell, and turn that paper gain into a real one. Until then, the whole gain, however large, is invisible to tax. This is why the questions of when to sell, and what to sell, are not only questions of investment. They are questions of tax, because selling is the act that brings the tax into being.

The same distinction gives losses their value. A realized loss, made real by selling something for less than it cost, can be set against realized gains. If a customer has a gain of five hundred from one sale and a loss of two hundred from another, they are taxed not on the five hundred, but on the three hundred that remains after the loss is subtracted. Losses offset gains. And if the losses in a year come to more than the gains, the excess is not simply wasted. A limited part of it can usually be set against other income, and whatever still remains can be carried forward to reduce tax in future years. This ability of losses to cancel gains is the quiet reason behind a strategy we will meet shortly.

### The Holding Period and the Lot

Not all realized gains are taxed in the same way, and here two threads from Chapter 8 finally show their purpose.

The first is time. How long a holding was owned before it was sold, from the day it was bought to the day it was sold, is called the holding period. In many tax systems, this length matters a great deal. A gain on something held only a short time, often less than about a year, is taxed at a higher rate, the same rate as ordinary earnings. A gain on something held longer is taxed at a lower rate, as a reward for holding for the long term. This is exactly why, back in Chapter 8, we insisted that every lot must remember the date it was bought. That date, set against the date of sale, decides the holding period, and the holding period decides the rate. The acquisition date we recorded was never mere bookkeeping. It was a tax fact waiting to be used.

The second thread is the choice of lot. Recall that a position is made of lots, each bought at its own price on its own date. When a customer sells part of a position, which lots are treated as sold changes the tax, and now we can see fully why. Different lots have different costs, so they produce different gains. And different lots have different dates, so they may fall on different sides of the line between short and long holding. By choosing which lots to sell, a customer, or a system acting for them, can shape the tax on a sale: selling lots with a higher cost to keep the gain small, or selling lots held long enough to earn the lower rate. The methods we named in Chapter 8, such as selling the oldest first, or naming particular lots, are, seen from here, tax choices. The lots and their dates, which seemed like fussy detail when we first met them, exist so that this shaping is possible.

## Income from Holdings

Selling is not the only way wealth is taxed. The second source needs no sale at all. It comes simply from holding.

Many investments pay their owners along the way. A share may pay a dividend, a portion of a company's profits handed to those who hold it. A bond, and cash held in the account, may pay interest. These payments are income, and income is generally taxed in the year it is received, whether or not the customer sells anything. A customer who buys shares and never sells them may still owe tax each year on the dividends those shares pay.

There are differences in how income is taxed that we will only touch. Some dividends are taxed at a lower rate, much like a long-held gain, while others are taxed as ordinary earnings. Some interest, such as that from certain public bonds, is free of some taxes altogether. The details vary widely by place. What matters to us is the plain fact that holding, on its own, produces taxable income, and a wealth system must track and report that income as carefully as it tracks gains.

One point deserves special care, because it joins two ideas we have already met. In Chapter 8 we saw that a dividend can be reinvested, used at once to buy a little more of the same investment, creating a new lot. It is tempting to think that a reinvested dividend, since the customer never touched the cash, is not taxed. This is wrong. A reinvested dividend is still income, and it is still taxed in the year it is received, even though the money went straight back into the investment. The customer owes tax on a dividend they never held in their hand. Reinvestment changes where the money goes. It does not change that the money was earned, and taxed.

## Managing the Tax

Because tax follows so closely from what a customer does, it can, within limits, be managed. A customer cannot change the rules, but they can choose their actions with the rules in mind. Two ideas show this most clearly, and they belong together, because the second exists to restrain the first.

### Tax-Loss Harvesting

We saw that a realized loss can be set against realized gains, reducing the tax owed. This gives rise to a deliberate practice. Suppose a customer has realized some gains during the year, on which tax will be owed. Suppose also that, among their holdings, something has fallen in value and now sits at a loss, though only on paper. The customer can sell that fallen holding, turning its paper loss into a realized one, and set that realized loss against their gains. Their taxable gain shrinks, and so does their tax. This deliberate selling of losers to capture their losses is called tax-loss harvesting.

There is a subtlety that makes it clever rather than merely sad. The customer may not actually wish to be rid of that investment. They sold it only for the tax benefit, not because they no longer wanted that kind of holding. So, having sold it, they buy something similar to take its place, keeping much the same position in the market while capturing the realized loss. They end up invested in nearly the same way as before, but with a loss captured that lowers their tax. Done across a whole portfolio, and across a year, this can save a meaningful amount, and it is one of the real services a wealth manager, or a wealth system, can provide.

### The Wash Sale Rule

Tax-loss harvesting has an obvious temptation built into it. If selling a holding at a loss and buying it straight back gives a tax benefit while leaving the customer in exactly the same position, why not do it constantly, capturing losses again and again with no real change? The tax authorities long ago saw this, and made a rule to stop it. That rule is the wash sale rule.

The wash sale rule says, in essence, that a customer may not claim a loss if they buy back the same investment, or one that is substantially identical, too soon around the sale. If the repurchase falls within a set window, often about a month before or after the sale, the loss is disallowed. It cannot be used to offset gains this year. The point of the rule is to insist that a loss counts only if the customer truly changed their position, not if they sold and rebought in a way that left them where they began.

A disallowed loss is not, however, destroyed. It is deferred. The amount of the disallowed loss is added to the cost of the newly bought shares, raising their cost basis, so that the benefit returns later, when those shares are finally sold. The loss is delayed, not lost. This is a small mercy, but a real complication, because a system must not only detect the wash sale but also adjust the cost basis of the replacement holding to carry the deferred loss forward.

The wash sale rule is one of the more demanding things a wealth system must handle, for two reasons. First, judging whether two investments are substantially identical is not always simple. Second, the rule can reach across a customer's different accounts, so that a sale in one account and a purchase in another, even months apart, must be seen together. This is exactly why the harvesting we just described is done with care: the customer sells the loser and buys something similar, but deliberately not identical, so as to capture the loss without breaking the rule. The rule and the strategy are two halves of one game, and a wealth system must understand both.

## It All Depends on the Account

Everything we have said so far, the gains and losses, the holding periods, the income, the harvesting and the wash sale, shares a hidden assumption. It all assumes that the tax applies now, as things happen. But whether it does apply now depends entirely on something we studied long ago: the kind of account the holding sits in. This is the promise we made in Chapter 6, now kept.

In a taxable account, everything in this chapter applies in full. Each sale is a taxable event. Each dividend is taxable income. The holding period matters, the choice of lot matters, the wash sale rule applies, and harvesting is worthwhile. The customer, or their system, must attend to all of it, all the time.

In a tax-advantaged account, almost all of it falls away. Recall from Chapter 6 that these accounts, such as those for retirement, are not taxed as things happen inside them. Their gains and their income are either taxed only later, when money is finally withdrawn, or, in the case of an after-tax account, never taxed again at all. Inside such an account, then, selling for a gain triggers no tax now. A dividend triggers no tax now. And so the whole apparatus of this chapter, the tracking of holding periods, the shaping of gains through lot choice, the harvesting of losses, the guarding against wash sales, largely does not apply, because there is no current tax to manage. It is replaced by the account's own rules, which we met in Chapter 6: the limits on what may be added, and the rules on when money may be taken out.

This is why we said, in Chapter 6, that the type of an account was not a label but a deep property. Here we see the full weight of it. The very same sale, of the very same holding, for the very same gain, is a taxable event in one account and a tax non-event in another. Before a wealth system can decide what tax an action carries, it must first ask a question that comes before all the others: which kind of account are we in?

## Reporting the Tax

There is a final duty that tax lays on a wealth system, and it is a heavy one. It is not enough to know the tax consequence of each event. All of it must be gathered, at the end of each year, into a record that the customer and the tax authority can use.

Through the year, taxable events pile up: sales with their gains and losses, dividends, interest. At year's end, the firm must gather them into tax documents, forms that summarise, for the customer, what they earned and what they realised over the year. In some countries these are known by names such as the 1099. The firm both gives these forms to the customer, so that the customer can complete their own tax return, and reports the same information directly to the tax authority, so that the two can be checked against each other.

Behind this lies a demand that has shaped much of what we studied in Chapter 8. For many holdings, a firm is required by law not only to report the proceeds of a sale, but the cost basis as well, so that the gain itself can be shown and checked. This means the careful tracking of cost, lot by lot, that we described is not merely a convenience for the customer. It is a legal obligation, owed to the tax authority. If a firm's record of what a holding cost is wrong, the gain it reports is wrong, and the tax is wrong. The insistence on getting cost basis right, which may have seemed excessive when we first met it, is revealed here as something a firm has no choice but to do. Tax reporting is where the quiet, exact record-keeping of wealth meets the hard requirement of the law.

## What the Settled Side Owes

We can now stand back and see what tax has shown us about the nature of wealth.

Notice, first, where tax lives. All through this book, the difficulty of wealth has come from its uncertain, external side: prices that move, values that will not settle, gains that exist only on paper and change by the minute. Tax has nothing to do with any of that. Tax does not care what a holding is worth today, or how much it has risen on paper. It cares only about what is real and settled: an actual sale, at an actual price, producing an actual gain; an actual dividend, actually received. Tax lives entirely on the certain, internal side of the seam we drew in Chapter 1. It is what the settled facts of wealth owe. The moving market creates no tax. Only the moment of realisation, when a paper gain is turned into a real one, brings tax into being. Tax follows from every crossing from the uncertain to the certain that we have traced through these chapters.

Notice, second, how tax has repaid our earlier care. When we insisted, chapters ago, that a position must be broken into lots, and that each lot must carry its cost and its date, it may have seemed like too much fuss over small things. Tax is the reason for all of it. The lots, the costs, the dates, the choice of which to sell: every piece of that fine detail exists so that the tax can be worked out correctly and reported truthfully. What looked like fussiness was foresight.

And notice, third, the shape we have seen so many times. The simple idea, I made money, so I owe tax on it, unfolded into a whole apparatus: tax only on what is realised, at rates that depend on how long a thing was held, reduced by losses, shaped by which lots are sold, applied to income as well as to gains, restrained by the wash sale rule, and switched almost entirely on or off by the kind of account. Once again, the plain surface of wealth hid a deep and careful structure beneath.

We have now followed a customer's wealth through nearly its whole life: where it is held, what it is, what it is worth, how it is traded, how it is measured, and how it is taxed. Through all of it, we have quietly assumed that the customer knows what they are doing, or has someone who does. But wealth is hard, as we have seen again and again, and few customers face it alone. Most rely on guidance, whether from a person or from a system, to make sense of these choices and to make them well. That guidance is itself a large part of the wealth domain. It is the subject of the next chapter, where we turn to advice and planning.
