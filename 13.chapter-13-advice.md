# Chapter 13: Advice and Planning

For twelve chapters we have studied the machinery of wealth. We have seen where it is held, what it is made of, what it is worth, how it is bought and sold, how it is measured, and how it is taxed. Through all of it, one figure has stood quietly in the background: the customer, the person whose wealth it is, trying to reach some goal in their life. In this chapter that person steps forward. We turn from the machinery to the decisions, and to the guidance by which those decisions are made. This guidance is called advice, and it is a large and human part of the wealth domain.

Advice exists for a simple reason, which this whole book has been demonstrating: wealth is hard. The choices are many, the rules are intricate, and the future is unknown. Few customers can weigh all of this well on their own. So most turn to help, from a professional adviser, or, increasingly, from software built to advise. The task of advice is to take everything we have studied, the accounts, the instruments, the allocation, the tax, and use it to help a customer decide what to do, and to do it well, in the service of what they are trying to achieve.

Notice the change of direction this brings. Performance, in Chapter 11, looked backward, judging how a portfolio had done. Advice looks forward. It is not about measuring the past but about shaping the future: setting a goal, building a plan to reach it, and keeping to that plan as the years and the markets move. And where earlier chapters described the parts of wealth, advice is where those parts are finally put to use, in the making of real decisions for a real person.

## It Begins With the Customer

Advice does not begin with the market. It begins with the customer. Before any investment is chosen, an adviser, whether a person or a program, must understand the one for whom the choosing is done.

Several things must be understood. First, the customer's goals: what they are actually saving for. It may be retirement, many years away. It may be a house, or a child's education, or an income to live on after work has ended. A goal has a size, the amount that will be needed, and a time, the point at which it will be needed. Second, the customer's means: what they have already, and what they can add over time. Third, and most delicate, the customer's tolerance for risk: how much fall in value they can bear, both in their circumstances and in their nerves. A young saver with a distant goal can accept large swings; an older one, near the moment of need, cannot afford them.

From this understanding, a plan is formed. The plan is the bridge between where the customer is and where they wish to be. Its central choice is the one we met in Chapter 8: the target allocation, the intended spread of the portfolio across kinds of investment. We can now see where that target comes from. It is not chosen at random, nor to seek the highest return. It is chosen to fit the customer, to give the best chance of reaching their goal within the risk they can bear. A distant goal and a strong nerve may call for a large share in equities, which can grow greatly but fall hard. A near goal, or a cautious customer, calls for more in steadier holdings. The plan turns the customer's life into a shape for their portfolio. Everything advice does afterward is in service of that plan.

## Projections: Planning Under Uncertainty

A plan raises an immediate question: will it work? Will investing in this way actually reach the goal in time? To answer, advice makes projections: estimates of how the portfolio might grow over the years ahead, to see whether it is likely to arrive where the customer needs it to be.

But here the deepest theme of this book returns, now pointed at the future. We have said, again and again, that the market cannot be known in advance. A projection cannot escape this. It cannot say what the portfolio will be worth in twenty years, because no one can. A projection is an estimate under assumptions, not a promise. This is a matter of honesty that advice must never forget, and that a wealth system must never let a customer forget either. A projected number is a possibility, not a fact of the future.

Because the future is uncertain, a good projection does not offer a single line. It offers a range. Rather than saying the portfolio will be worth a certain amount, it explores many possible futures, some kind to the customer and some cruel, and reports what tends to happen across them. It might find that in most of these futures the goal is comfortably reached, but in the worst the customer falls short. This turns the plan from a single guess into an honest picture of the odds: not this will happen, but this is likely, this is possible, and this, though unwelcome, could occur. Planning under uncertainty does not pretend the uncertainty away. It measures it, and lets the customer see it, so that the plan can be made strong enough to survive a future that will not be as tidy as any single projection.

## Rebalancing: Keeping to the Plan

A plan, once made, does not keep itself. We saw why in Chapter 8. Because a portfolio's allocation is measured by value, and values move, the actual allocation drifts on its own, away from the target the plan set. A portfolio built to hold half in equities may, after a good year for equities, hold well over half, and so carry more risk than the plan intended, without a single deliberate choice. Left alone, a portfolio slowly stops matching its plan.

Rebalancing is the act of bringing it back. To rebalance is to sell some of what has grown too large a share, and buy more of what has shrunk, until the allocation matches the target again. It is how a plan is kept alive against the drift of the market. Its purpose is not to seek gains, but to hold the portfolio's risk where the customer chose it to be.

Rebalancing, though, is not free, and here two earlier chapters press in. To rebalance usually means to sell, and selling, as we learned in Chapter 12, can create a realized gain, and with it a tax to pay. Selling also carries the costs of trading we met in Chapter 10. So rebalancing sets one good against another: the good of staying true to the plan, against the cost, in tax and in fees, of the selling it requires. Good advice weighs these carefully. It may choose to rebalance only when the drift has grown large enough to matter, rather than at every small movement. It may rebalance cleverly, by steering new money that the customer adds toward the holdings that have fallen behind, correcting the drift without selling anything at all. It may do its rebalancing inside a tax-advantaged account, where, as we saw, selling triggers no tax. Rebalancing is where the plan meets the machinery, and where a single decision to stay with the plan ripples outward into trades and tax.

## Model Portfolios

An adviser could design a fresh portfolio for every customer, choosing each holding by hand. For a few customers, this is done. But most wealth is not managed that way, because it would not scale, and because it would be hard to keep consistent. Instead, advice relies heavily on the model portfolio.

A model portfolio is a ready-made design: a target allocation, often with particular holdings named, built for a certain kind of customer. There might be a cautious model, holding mostly steady investments, for those near their goal or nervous of loss. There might be a bold model, holding mostly equities, for those far from their goal and able to bear risk. And several in between. Rather than design from nothing, an adviser matches the customer to the model that fits their plan, and the customer's portfolio is built and kept to match that model.

We can sketch the shape of such a model.

```
ModelPortfolio
  name           the name of the model, such as cautious or growth
  targets        the intended share of each kind of holding
                   for example: equities 40, fixed income 50, cash 10
```

As before, this is a plain description, not code to run. The value of models is twofold. They bring consistency: every customer assigned to a model is invested in the same considered way, rather than each depending on the separate judgment of a single adviser. And they bring reach: when the firm decides to change a model, perhaps shifting its intended allocation as conditions change, every portfolio that follows that model can be moved to match, all together. A model portfolio is a single design serving many customers, and it lets careful thinking, done once, be applied at scale. For a wealth system, a model is a real thing to be stored, linked to the accounts that follow it, and used to drive the rebalancing we have just described.

## The Advice Relationship

Advice is not only a set of activities. It is a relationship, and the relationship itself is part of the domain a wealth system must represent. A customer has an adviser, or a team of them, who guides their wealth. This link, between the one advised and the one advising, is a real thing to be recorded: who advises whom, and with what authority.

Two features of this relationship are worth drawing out. The first is that advice often reaches beyond a single account, or even a single person. A customer's goals belong to their whole financial life, not to one account. Retirement is not saved for in one place alone; it may draw on several accounts at once. For this reason, advice frequently works at the level of a household: a family, or a group of related people and their accounts, considered together. The plan is a household plan. The allocation is judged across all the household's holdings. The tax is considered over the household as a whole. A wealth system that supports advice must be able to gather a household's many accounts into one view, and reason about them together. This is the same joining-together we will meet again in the final chapter, on reporting.

The second feature is the authority the relationship carries, and it deserves its own discussion, because it changes everything about how advice is given and how a system must support it.

### Who Holds the Power to Act

When an adviser recommends a course of action, who actually decides to take it? The answer defines the relationship, and it comes in two forms that we first named in Chapter 6.

In a non-discretionary relationship, the adviser may recommend, but the customer must agree to each decision before it is carried out. The adviser can say what they think should be done, but they cannot do it alone. Every trade waits for the customer's yes. The power to act stays firmly with the customer, and the adviser is a guide, not a hand.

In a discretionary relationship, the customer has handed the adviser authority to act on their behalf, within agreed bounds, without asking each time. The adviser can buy and sell as their judgment directs, and the customer learns of it afterward. Here the power to act has been delegated. The customer has chosen to be led, trusting the adviser to act in their interest.

This difference runs deep, and it reaches into the system that supports the advice. A system for non-discretionary advice must be built to present recommendations and to capture the customer's approval before anything happens; nothing may be done until the yes is recorded. A system for discretionary advice must be able to act directly, and to manage many accounts against their models at once, since it need not pause for each customer. The difference also carries great weight in the rules, as we will see in the next chapter. An adviser who may act alone, with another's money, holds a serious power, and the law places a correspondingly serious duty upon them. Where the power to act sits is not a mere setting. It shapes the relationship, the software, and the responsibility all at once.

### When the Adviser Is Software

Increasingly, the adviser is not a person at all, but a program. Automated advice, often called robo-advice, takes the whole process we have described in this chapter and encodes it in software. It asks the customer about their goals and their tolerance for risk, usually through a set of questions. From the answers, it places the customer into a suitable model portfolio. It then builds the portfolio, and keeps it, rebalancing as it drifts and, in some cases, harvesting tax losses along the way, all without a human hand.

Automated advice is discretionary by its nature, since the program acts on its own. Its great effect is to make advice cheap and available to many who could never afford a personal adviser. But for our purposes, there is a more striking observation. Everything this chapter has described, the understanding of goals, the choosing of an allocation, the projection of outcomes, the rebalancing, the tax-aware selling, is exactly what such software does. In other words, to build automated advice is to build, in code, the whole of this chapter. The reader who is constructing a wealth system may find that advice is not a topic to be studied from a distance, but the very thing they are being asked to create. Advice, encoded, is software, and it draws together nearly everything in this book.

## The Machinery in the Service of a Goal

We can now see advice for what it is: the place where all the machinery of wealth is finally put to work for a person.

Every earlier chapter described a part. The account held things; the instruments were the things; the positions recorded them; the prices valued them; the trades changed them; performance judged them; tax claimed a share of them. Each was studied on its own. Advice is where they cease to be separate topics and become, together, the means of helping someone reach a goal in their life. When an adviser sets an allocation, they are using the instruments and the account. When they project the future, they are facing the uncertainty of prices. When they rebalance, they are ordering trades and weighing tax. Advice does not add a new piece to wealth so much as it uses every piece at once, with a purpose the pieces alone did not have.

That purpose returns us to where this chapter began: the customer, and their goal. All the intricate structure we have studied exists, in the end, so that a person can retire, or buy a home, or leave something behind. This is easy to forget among the lots and the yields and the settlement dates, but advice never can. It must hold, always, the human aim behind the machine.

And yet, precisely because advice holds such power over another person's future and money, it cannot be left to good intentions alone. An adviser who may act with a customer's savings, who may recommend what a customer cannot easily judge, holds a trust that can be abused as well as honoured. Society has not left this trust unguarded. Around all of wealth, and most tightly around advice, there is a body of rules, meant to protect the customer from the very powers we have just described. Those rules are the subject of the next chapter. Having seen how much advice can do, we turn to how it is held to account.
