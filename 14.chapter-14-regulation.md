# Chapter 14: Regulation

The last chapter ended on a note of unease. Advice, we saw, gives one party great power over another's money and future. An adviser may act with a customer's savings, and may recommend what the customer cannot easily judge for themselves. This is a relationship built on trust, and trust can be betrayed. This chapter is about how society tries to prevent that betrayal. It is about regulation: the body of rules that governs wealth, and, most of all, that protects the customer.

Regulation is unlike the subjects of the chapters before it. Those were parts of wealth, things that could be pointed to: an account, an instrument, a trade. Regulation is not a part. It is a set of demands laid over every part. It shapes how an account is opened, what advice may be given, how a trade is carried out, and what must be recorded at each step. A wealth system does not have a corner marked regulation. Instead, regulation runs through the whole of it, touching nearly everything the system does.

Before we go on, the same honest caution we gave with tax applies here. The rules of wealth differ from country to country, and they change over time. There are many regulators, and many bodies of law, and we will not learn any one of them. What we will learn is the shape that holds broadly: the kinds of duty that wealth regulation imposes, the reasons behind them, and the marks they leave on a wealth system. That shape is remarkably consistent across the world, even where the details are not.

## Why the Customer Needs Protecting

To understand wealth regulation, we must first see why the customer is in a weak position, for nearly every rule exists to answer some part of that weakness.

Three things put the customer at a disadvantage. The first is what is at stake. For many people, their investments are their savings for old age, or for a home, or for their children. A wrong choice is not a small loss but a life changed. The second is knowledge. Wealth is hard, as this whole book has shown. Most customers cannot judge for themselves whether an investment is wise, whether a fee is fair, or whether advice is sound. They must trust someone who knows far more than they do. The third, and the sharpest, is conflict. The firm that advises a customer often makes money in ways that can pull against the customer's own interest. It may earn more from selling one product than another. It may have its own products to sell. It may be paid for steering business in a certain direction. In each case, what is best for the firm and what is best for the customer may quietly differ.

Put these together, and the danger is plain. A customer with much to lose, little ability to judge, must rely on a firm that has reasons of its own to serve itself. This is the imbalance that wealth regulation exists to correct. It cannot make the customer an expert, and it cannot remove every conflict. But it can place duties on the firm, force important facts into the open, and hold the firm to account, so that the customer's trust is not left undefended. Almost every rule we are about to meet is a response to one of these three weaknesses: the stakes, the knowledge, or the conflict.

## Knowing the Customer

We first met the duty to know the customer in Chapter 6, when an account was opened. We can now see that it runs deeper than it first appeared, and that it serves two quite different purposes.

The first purpose is to know who the customer truly is. Before a firm takes someone's money, it must establish that they are who they claim to be, a real and identified person, and not, for instance, someone using wealth to hide the proceeds of crime, or to move money for forbidden ends. This side of knowing the customer is about safety and honesty in the system as a whole. It requires verifying identity, and checking the customer against lists of those who are barred or sanctioned. It does not end at the opening, either. A firm must keep watching for activity that looks wrong, and must act when it does. This is a heavy duty, and much of it is shared with retail banking, though wealth applies it with particular care.

The second purpose is entirely different, and it is the one that connects to advice. To advise a customer well, and lawfully, a firm must understand the customer's situation: their income and wealth, their goals, their experience with investing, and how much risk they can bear. This is the very understanding we described at the start of the last chapter. What we treated there as good practice is, in fact, a requirement of the rules. A firm cannot judge whether advice suits a customer without first knowing the customer, so the law demands that this knowledge be gathered, recorded, and kept current. The profile built at the outset is not a formality. It is the ground on which every later judgment about suitable advice will stand.

## The Standard of Advice

Once a firm knows its customer, and comes to advise them, a question arises that lies at the very heart of wealth regulation: how good must the advice be? To what standard is the firm held when it tells a customer what to do with their money? The answer has changed over time, and it comes in a rising ladder of duty.

### Suitable, or in the Customer's Interest?

The older and weaker standard is suitability. Under it, a recommendation must be suitable for the customer: it must fit their situation, their goals, and the risk they can bear. An adviser may not recommend something plainly wrong for the person in front of them, such as a risky, volatile investment for someone who has said they need safety and cannot bear loss.

Suitability protects against bad advice. But it leaves a subtle gap, and the gap matters. Among several investments that would all be suitable, an adviser under this standard may recommend the one that pays the adviser the most, so long as it is still suitable for the customer. The advice is not wrong, exactly. It simply is not the best available for the customer, because it was chosen partly to serve the adviser. Suitability, in other words, guards against harm, but not against a quiet preference for the firm's own gain.

For this reason, many places have moved to a stronger standard: that a recommendation must be in the customer's best interest, not merely suitable for them. Under this higher standard, an adviser can no longer choose a suitable-but-self-serving option and defend it as good enough. They must recommend what is genuinely best for the customer, and they must confront the conflicts that might tempt them elsewhere. In some countries this stronger duty is set down in named rules; the details vary, but the direction is the same everywhere. The minimum is being raised, from advice that merely fits to advice that truly serves.

### The Fiduciary Duty

At the top of the ladder stands the strongest duty of all: the fiduciary duty. To be a fiduciary is to be bound, in law, to put the customer's interest ahead of one's own, always and without exception. A fiduciary must act with loyalty and with care. They must avoid conflicts where they can, and where they cannot, they must reveal them fully and manage them for the customer's benefit, never their own. It is the highest obligation one party can owe another over money.

Here a thread from the last chapter is drawn tight. We saw that a customer may hand an adviser the power to act on their behalf, without approval for each decision, in what we called a discretionary relationship. We said then that this power carried a serious duty. Now we can name it. The more power and trust a customer places in an adviser, the higher the standard the law tends to demand. An adviser who merely suggests, leaving every decision to the customer, may be held to a lighter duty. An adviser who acts alone with the customer's money, deciding and trading on their behalf, is often held to the fiduciary standard, the highest there is, precisely because the customer has made themselves so vulnerable to them. Power and duty rise together. This is why, in Chapter 13, we said that where the power to act sits was not a mere setting. It helps to decide the very standard of conduct the law will require.

## Conflicts and Disclosure

We have seen that conflicts of interest sit behind much of this. The firm has reasons to serve itself that may pull against the customer. Regulation has two main tools for dealing with a conflict. It may forbid it outright, ruling that certain arrangements are simply not allowed. Or, far more often, it may require that the conflict be disclosed.

Disclosure is the duty to tell the customer, plainly and before they act, the things they need to know in order to judge for themselves. Chief among these are three: the risks of what is being recommended, the costs and fees they will pay, and the conflicts of interest that bear on the advice. The reasoning is that a customer who is told, clearly and in advance, that a fee will be charged, or that the firm earns more from this product than another, is a customer who can weigh the advice fully aware. Disclosure does not remove a conflict. It exposes it, and trusts the informed customer to account for it.

For a wealth system, disclosure is not merely a document to be shown. It carries a real obligation to act and to record. The required disclosures, the terms of the relationship, the schedules of fees, the warnings of risk, must be presented to the customer at the right moments, often before an account is opened or an action is taken. And the customer's acceptance of them must frequently be captured and kept: a record that this customer was shown these terms, and agreed to them, on this date. This is why the acceptance of terms is a real event in the life of a wealth relationship, one the system must present, capture, and store, so that the firm can later show not only what it disclosed, but that the customer knowingly agreed.

## The Duty to Prove It

Running through everything we have said is a demand so pervasive that it deserves to be named on its own. It is not enough, under wealth regulation, for a firm to do the right thing. The firm must be able to prove that it did. This turns much of a wealth system into an apparatus for keeping records, not only of what happened, but of why, and of whether the rules were followed.

Consider what must be provable. That the customer's identity was checked, and their profile gathered, before advice was given. That each recommendation was suitable, or in the customer's best interest, and on what basis it was judged so. That the required disclosures were made, and the customer's agreement recorded. That when a trade was placed, the firm sought the best reasonably available terms for the customer, the duty of best execution we first met in Chapter 10. Each of these is not only a thing to be done, but a thing to be shown, later, to a regulator or an auditor who may ask the firm to account for its conduct long after the fact.

This has a deep effect on how a wealth system must be built, though the details of the building belong to another book. Records cannot be an afterthought. The system must, as a matter of course, capture and keep a full record of what it did and why: who was told what, and when; why each recommendation was made; what was disclosed and agreed; how each order was handled. The careful record-keeping we have praised throughout this book, of costs, of lots, of movements, finds here its sternest reason. It is not only for the customer's benefit, and not only for the tax authority, as we saw in Chapter 12. It is so that the firm can stand before those who oversee it and show, from its records, that it kept faith with the customer. In wealth, to be unable to prove that a rule was followed is very nearly the same as to have broken it.

## The Protection of Trust

We can now stand back and see what regulation is, beneath its many rules.

Throughout this book, the difficulty of wealth has come from one source above all: uncertainty. The market moves, values will not settle, the future cannot be known. Against that uncertainty, no rule can help; it is simply the nature of the thing. But the customer faces a second danger, quite apart from the market, and it is this second danger that regulation answers. It is the danger of the trusting made vulnerable to the powerful: a customer who cannot judge, with much to lose, placing their savings in the hands of a firm that has reasons of its own. The market's risk falls on everyone alike, and no one governs it. This other risk falls on the weaker party, and it can be governed. Regulation is how society governs it.

Seen this way, every rule we have met is a single idea in many forms. Know the customer, so that advice can fit them. Hold advice to a standard, rising with the power the adviser holds, so that trust is repaid with loyalty. Disclose the risks, the costs, the conflicts, so that the customer may judge with full knowledge. And keep the records to prove it all, so that the duty is not merely felt but shown. Each is a way of protecting the trust that wealth requires, and without which it could not function. For a wealth system, this means that regulation cannot be a feature added at the end. It is a condition on nearly every action the system takes, woven through the whole, exactly as the trust it guards is woven through the whole of wealth.

We have now studied wealth from almost every side: where it is held, what it is, what it is worth, how it moves, how it is judged, how it is taxed, how it is advised, and how it is governed. One task remains, and it is the one that gathers all the others together. Everything we have studied must, in the end, be shown: to the customer, who wishes to see what they have and how it fares; to the tax authority and the regulator, who require their accounts; and increasingly, in a single view that draws a whole financial life into one place. That gathering and showing is the subject of our final chapter, on reporting.
