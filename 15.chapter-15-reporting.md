# Chapter 15: Reporting and Aggregation

We arrive at the last of our subjects, and it is a fitting one to end on, because it gathers all the others together. Everything we have studied in this book produces information. An account holds positions; the positions have values; the values change; trades are made; performance is measured; tax is calculated; duties are met. All of this must, in the end, be shown. Reporting is the showing. It is how the scattered facts of a customer's wealth are gathered, formed into a picture that can be understood or checked, and delivered to those who need to see it.

This makes reporting the natural close of our study, because to report on wealth is to draw on the whole of it. A statement of an account calls upon the holdings, the prices, the trades, and the performance, all at once. It is not a new part of wealth so much as a bringing-together of every part we have already met. And so, in describing how wealth is reported, we will find ourselves revisiting, one last time, nearly everything this book has taught.

We will look first at why wealth is reported at all, and to whom. We will study the statement, the familiar summary of an account. We will see the many documents that accumulate over a relationship, and the need to keep and show them. And we will end with the largest and most forward-looking idea of all: the gathering of a customer's whole financial life, scattered across many places, into a single view. That last idea will carry us back, at the very end, to where this book began.

## Two Reasons to Report

Wealth is reported for two quite different reasons, to two quite different audiences, and it helps to separate them from the start.

The first audience is the customer. A customer wants to see, and to understand, what is happening with their money. What do they hold? What is it worth? How has it done? What do they owe? Reporting to the customer is in the service of understanding and decision. Its aim is to make the complex picture of a portfolio clear enough that a person can grasp it, judge it, and act on it. A report for the customer succeeds when it turns a mass of facts into understanding.

The second audience is the authorities: the tax authority, and the regulator. These do not report for understanding, but demand reports as a matter of law. We have already met both duties. In Chapter 12 we saw that a firm must report a customer's gains, losses, and income to the tax authority each year. In Chapter 14 we saw that a firm must keep records and be able to show, to those who oversee it, that it followed the rules. Reporting to the authorities is in the service of obligation. Its aim is not to help anyone understand, but to satisfy a requirement that the firm cannot refuse.

The same underlying facts feed both. A customer's holdings and their gains are the raw material of the statement they read and of the tax document filed on their behalf. But the two are shaped differently, because their purposes differ. One is made to be understood; the other, to comply. As we look at the forms reporting takes, it is worth keeping in mind which of these two ends each one serves.

## The Statement

The most familiar report of all is the statement. A statement is a summary of an account over a period of time, sent regularly, perhaps each month or each quarter. It is the customer's regular, settled record of the account.

A good statement gathers several of the things we have studied into one place. It lists the holdings, the positions we met in Chapter 8, each with its quantity and its value. It shows the total worth of the account. It records the activity over the period: the trades made, the money added and withdrawn, the income received, the corporate actions applied. And it often shows performance, how the account has done over the period and beyond. In a single document, the statement draws together the holdings, the prices, the trades, and the measurement of return.

One feature of the statement is worth pausing on, because it returns us to a distinction from Chapter 9. A statement is fixed to a date. It shows the account as it stood at the close of a chosen day, and to do so it uses not the live, moving prices, but the settled closing prices of that day. This is quite different from what a customer sees when they open an application and watch their portfolio move through the day. That live view belongs to the restless, uncertain world of the market stream. The statement belongs to the other world we described, the official, settled world of the closing price. The application shows the customer what their wealth is doing now, changing by the second. The statement shows what their wealth was, exactly, at a fixed moment that will not change. Both are true. They are simply the two faces of value, the live and the settled, that we first drew apart long ago, now appearing as two different kinds of report.

## A Life of Documents

The statement is not the only thing a customer receives. Over the life of a relationship, documents accumulate steadily, each recording some event or duty. Every trade may bring a confirmation, a record of exactly what was bought or sold, at what price, and when. Each year brings the tax documents of Chapter 12. The opening of the relationship, and its course, bring the disclosures and agreements of Chapter 14, the terms accepted, the risks and costs laid out. There are notices of corporate actions, and letters, and more. Wealth, it turns out, generates a great deal of paper, even when no paper is involved.

All of these documents must be kept, and made available. For the customer, they must be reachable: a person may need to find last year's tax document, or the confirmation of a trade, or the terms they once agreed to. So a wealth system commonly gathers a customer's documents into one organised place, where they can be stored and found. But there is a second reason to keep them, which we have already met. In Chapter 14 we saw that a firm must be able to prove what it did. These same documents are that proof. The confirmation shows what was traded; the record of accepted terms shows what was disclosed and agreed. A document is therefore two things at once: a service to the customer, who may wish to see it, and a record for the firm, which may one day have to produce it. The gathering and keeping of documents serves the person and the law together.

## Aggregation and the Whole Financial Life

We come, at last, to the largest idea in reporting, and one that looks toward the future of wealth. Everything we have said so far assumes we are reporting on the accounts a customer holds with a single firm. But a customer's wealth is rarely all in one place.

### Gathering the Scattered

A person may hold a brokerage account with one firm, a retirement account with another, and everyday banking with a third. Their wealth is scattered across many institutions, each holding a piece, none holding the whole. Any single firm can report only on its own piece. Yet the customer does not experience their wealth in pieces. They have one financial life, and they increasingly wish to see all of it together, in one place. The gathering of a customer's holdings from many sources into a single picture is called aggregation.

Aggregation is difficult, and its difficulty is a familiar kind. The data must be brought in from many outside sources, each an institution the firm does not control, and each describing its holdings in its own way, in its own form. Before the pieces can be shown together, they must be reconciled: matched, made consistent, and translated into a common shape. Here an old problem returns. To combine a holding reported by one institution with a holding of the same instrument reported by another, we must be sure they are indeed the same instrument, which sends us back to the careful matching of identity we studied in Chapter 7. And because the data comes from outside, it carries the uncertainty of all external things, as prices did in Chapter 9. It may arrive late, or incomplete, or in a form that must be interpreted. Aggregation joins the firm's own certain records with a wider world of data that is messier and less sure. Doing it well, so that the combined picture is trustworthy, is one of the harder tasks in modern wealth software, and much effort goes into the connections and services that make it possible.

### One Financial Life

When aggregation succeeds, the customer is given something powerful: a single, unified view of their whole financial life. On one screen, they see everything they own, wherever it is held, gathered into one total, one allocation, one measure of how they are doing across all of it. The scattered pieces become a whole.

And here, at the end of our long study, this book closes a circle it opened at the very beginning. Let us think back to Part 1. There, we took the whole of banking and divided it into three categories: retail, the holding and moving of money; lending, the borrowing of it; and wealth, the owning of assets that change in value. We divided it so that we could understand it, one category at a time. But even then, we noticed something. We said that the customer does not see these divisions. To the customer, their everyday account, their loan, and their investments are not three separate worlds. They are one financial life.

The unified view is where that truth is honoured. A complete picture of a customer's finances may bring together, on a single screen, the everyday accounts of retail, the loans of lending, and the investments of wealth, all at once. The three categories we so carefully separated, in order to study them, are joined back together, because that is how the customer lives them. We divided wealth to understand it. We aggregate it to serve the person whose wealth it is. The division was for us, the builders. The reunion is for them.

There is a quiet lesson in this, worth carrying away. The categories, the parts, the careful separations that fill this book are tools of understanding, not truths of experience. A customer never thinks in positions and lots and settlement dates. They think of their home, their retirement, their children. All the intricate structure we have studied exists, in the end, to serve that simple human view, and the unified picture is where the intricate structure and the simple view finally meet.

## A Last Word

We have reached the end of our map of wealth.

We began, many chapters ago, by placing wealth within the whole of banking, as one of three ways a bank serves its customers, and the hardest of them to build for. We then entered wealth alone, and studied it part by part. We saw where it is held, in accounts of many kinds. We saw what fills those accounts: the instruments, from simple shares to intricate bonds. We saw how holdings are recorded, in positions and their lots, and how their true worth is a matter both certain and uncertain at once. We faced the moving market from which all value comes, and followed a trade through its long life into that market. We measured how a portfolio has done, and found that even this had no single answer. We dealt with tax, and with the rules that guard the customer, and with the advice that turns all of it toward a human goal. And now, at the last, we have gathered it all back together, into a single picture of a single financial life.

Through all of it, one idea has returned again and again, and it is the idea to carry away above all others. Wealth is hard because its truth lives outside, in a market that moves without pause and can never be fully known. From that one fact flow the many difficulties we have met: the bundle of prices, the drifting values, the paper gains, the two kinds of return, the moment of realisation that turns the uncertain into the settled. Around this core of uncertainty stand the other demands: the web of parts that touch and depend on one another, and the trust that must be protected by rules. To understand wealth is to hold all of this at once.

This book has described that domain. It has not told us how to build for it, how to lay out the code, draw the boundaries, or choose the structures that would turn this understanding into working software. That was deliberate. The domain had first to be seen clearly, on its own, before the question of building could be asked well. That question, of how the shape of this domain becomes the shape of a system, is the subject of the book that follows this one. For now, we have done what we set out to do. We have drawn the map. The building begins from here.
